# 1820s ARGENTINE SPANISH — STYLE GUIDE & AUDIT RULES

*Companion to the campaign. Use when writing or auditing any read-aloud block, NPC dialogue, or in-fiction document.*

---

## 1. THE WINDOW WE'RE WRITING IN

The campaign is set in **1821**, in the immediate post-independence Río de la Plata. Buenos Aires is a colonial port becoming a republican capital under Bernardino Rivadavia's reforms. The pampas south of the Salado are a contested frontier; San Ruiz and the estancias around Azul are the literal edge of the Spanish-speaking world.

This means three coexisting registers, and we should be able to tell them apart on the page:

1. **Rural gaucho / pampa speech** — the foreman, ranch hands, *pulperos*, frontier women, Tomás, Rosa, Dionisia, Inocencia. Voseo, syncope, dropped *de*, rural lexicon, occasional archaism.
2. **Urban porteño / educated speech** — Don Eusebio, Don Eladio Saráchaga, Captain Galíndez, Doña Mercedes, Padre Albarrán, Aldao Quirce, the *vigilantes urbanos*. Voseo present but cleaner, full grammar, Castilian inflection still audible in older speakers.
3. **Indigenous / mestizo bilingual speech** — Kuyen, Nahuel, Héctor, Marta, the Cursed Gaucho. Spanish as a working language with code-switching to Mapudungun or Tehuelche; cleaner syntax than gauchos, but with cadences and metaphors that come from elsewhere.

We do **not** write a generic neutral Spanish. Each NPC sits somewhere on this map and the dialogue should read that placement.

---

## 2. VOSEO — THE NON-NEGOTIABLE

Río de la Plata Spanish was already firmly *voseante* in 1821. **Tú** is wrong for in-fiction speech almost everywhere in the campaign. Use **vos** for informal singular and **usted** for formal/respectful singular; plural is **ustedes** (never *vosotros* — Argentina has never used it).

| Form | Modern (avoid) | 1820s correct |
|---|---|---|
| You (informal sg.) | tú tienes | **vos tenés** |
| You (informal sg.) | tú sabes | **vos sabés** |
| You (informal sg.) | tú puedes | **vos podés** |
| Imperative | ven aquí | **vení acá** |
| Imperative | dime | **decime** |
| Imperative | toma | **tomá** |
| Imperative | mira | **mirá** |
| You (plural) | vosotros tenéis | **ustedes tienen** |
| Your (informal sg.) | tu casa | **tu casa** *(possessive is the same)* |

**When to use *usted*:**
- Speaking to an elder, a priest, a patrón, or a stranger you owe respect to.
- Tomás → Don Eusebio: *usted*. Don Eusebio → Tomás: *vos*.
- Rosa → the investigators on first meeting: *usted*. After they've shared *mate* and she trusts them: drifts to *vos*.
- Kuyen → outsiders: *usted* until trust; *vos* after.
- Padre Albarrán → anyone: *usted* in confession, *vos* in argument.
- Mercedes → players: *vos* (she's brought them in as peers); *usted* to Saráchaga, Galíndez (rank).
- El Patrón → anyone: *usted*. He keeps formal distance because formality is part of his geometry.

**When to mix:** Older characters (Saráchaga, Don Eusebio, Mercedes) sometimes slip into Castilian *tú*-forms in moments of high emotion or quotation. This is fine — flag it as a *register break*, not an error.

## 3. RURAL GAUCHO LEXICON & SYNTAX (PAMPA REGISTER)

| Rural form | Standard equivalent | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **ansí** | así | Rural archaism. Use for older speakers. |
| **naides** | nadie | Same. |
| **güeno** | bueno | Use sparingly — too much is parody. |
| **dende** | desde | Rural archaism. |
| **mesmo** | mismo | Rural archaism. |

| **pa'** | para | Rural syncope. *"Pa'l este"* = "para el este." |

| **bajío / cañadón / hondonada** | depresión del terreno | Pampa terrain words. |
| **pampa / pago / campo** | — | All period-correct for "the country." *"En el pago"* = "where I'm from." |
| **luz mala** | — | Folkloric, period. |

| **vizcacha / vizcachera** | — | Pampa rodent + its burrow. |

| **cebar (mate)** | preparar (mate) | Period-correct verb. |
| **tomar pa' (un rumbo)** | dirigirse hacia | Rural directional. *"Tomó pa'l este"* = "headed east." |
| **arrimar(se) / arrimo** | acercarse | *"Yo no me arrimo."* — *"I don't go near it."* |
| **vara** | yard / ~83 cm | Period unit. |
| **cuadra** | block / ~120 m | Period unit. |
| **legua** | league / ~5 km | Period unit. |

**Syntax tells (gaucho register):**
- Drop **de** before **que**: *"Antes que abrieran"* (vs. *"antes de que abrieran"*). Either is correct rural speech; the dropped *de* is more vivid.
- Tail *nomás*: *"Llegó, nomás."*
- Diminutive **-ito/-ita** in tender or dismissive use: *"solito,"* *"despacito,"* *"un ratito."*
- Diminutive **-ico** appears in some rural speech but is rarer in the Río de la Plata than in the Andean north — use very sparingly.
- **Le** as polite-singular indirect object: *"Le rasqué algo en el otro lado."* (Tomás to a player.) Standard rural courtesy.

## 4. URBAN PORTEÑO REGISTER (BUENOS AIRES, 1821)

Buenos Aires is a port that has just started staging a republican intelligentsia. Saráchaga, Galíndez, Mercedes, and Albarrán speak a Spanish that is still audibly Castilian — older speakers may seseo, may use *vosotros* in writing they were taught in (in spoken form: never), may quote Latin.

**Tells:**
- Slower, more deliberate sentence rhythms.
- Subjunctive used in full: *"Cuando me conteste, voy a saber."*
- Latin tags from the educated: *"sub signo magno,"* *"hic manet,"* *"ad maiorem Dei gloriam."*
- French loans from the new university crowd: *"Encyclopédie,"* *"frac"* (a coat cut), *"boudoir."* These are signals of Rivadavian modernity and should be rare and pointed.
- The municipal police are **vigilantes urbanos** (the Rivadavia-era body, formally constituted 1821–1822). Not "policía" generically.
- *Cabildo* still in use — it is the seat of municipal government and clock-keeping.
- *Manzana* (city block, sg.) is correct for the street grid.

**Don Eusebio's voice** (older patrón, rural-rich): blends rural tells (uses *facón*, *pampa*, *carajo*) with Castilian gravitas (full conjugations, no *naides* or *güeno*). He has been told all his life that he is the master of his patch and his speech reflects that. He is *not* a gaucho. He hires gauchos.

## 5. INDIGENOUS / MESTIZO REGISTER

Kuyen and the curanderas of San Ruiz speak Spanish as a *second* working language. Their tells are:
- Cleaner syntax than rural gauchos (they learned it in mission schools or from elders who learned it formally).
- Spare use of pampa lexicon (they don't say *carajo* or *güeno*; they may say *mate*, *facón* by adoption).
- Code-switching to Mapudungun for ritual or emotion, with a translation gloss in parens. Example: *"Lo de acá se cerró. Lo de allá no."*
- Slow cadence; complete sentences; metaphors that come from the land (river, deer, condor) rather than from the saint-calendar.

The Cursed Gaucho is mestizo and switches registers strategically. He uses gaucho speech when in cover, educated Spanish when establishing authority, and Mapudungun when grieving. **Don't write him as a single voice** — the shift is the character.

## 6. ANACHRONISM BLACKLIST — DO NOT USE

These are post-1821, mostly post-1880, and will mark the campaign as written by ear if they appear.

**Lunfardo (Italian-influenced Buenos Aires slang, 1880s onward) — never:**
- guita, mango, plata (for money — *plata* is fine for the metal, but using *plata* as slang for *money* feels Lunfardo; use **dineros, monedas, reales, pesos fuertes** instead)
- laburar, laburo
- pibe, piba, pibita
- mina (woman), milonga (dance — the *word* is OK by 1820s but the dance-floor sense is later), tango (musical genre — does not exist)
- macanudo, copado, posta, joya (as adjectives)
- afanar (to steal, in slang sense)
- bondi (bus), subte (subway), boliche (bar/club, this sense)
- chabón, chabona
- yirar, mufa
- gil, gilada
- rajar (to leave, slang sense)
- tomar el palo

**Voseo modern variants — avoid:**
- *vosotros* (Castilian plural — never used in River Plate)
- "tú tienes" / "tú sabes" / "te quiero" — write *vos tenés / vos sabés / te quiero* (the last is the same)

**Anglicisms and modern technical terms — never:**
- ok / okey / no problema
- bicicleta, tren, telégrafo (no telegraph in BA until 1857), teléfono, automóvil, película
- moderno (used in modern sense; 1820s sense is closer to *nuevo / actual*)
- "te tengo una sorpresa" (modern phrasing); use *"te tengo algo que mostrar"*

**Modern political/conceptual vocabulary — avoid:**
- liberalismo, conservadurismo (these terms exist but are emerging; use sparingly and only for the most educated speakers)
- "la oligarquía" — post-1900 framing; do not use
- "el régimen" in the modern sense
- "los derechos humanos" — anachronistic concept

**Catholic / clerical Spanish — period-fine to keep formal:**
- Latin tags (sub signo magno, hic manet, ad maiorem) are correct in priestly mouths
- *vuestra merced / vuesa merced* — already rare by 1821 except in the most formal letters; **use sparingly** as a stylistic choice for very old speakers; do not require it
- "Don / Doña" + first name is universal and required for any patrón/lady

## 8. THE THREE NAMED PRAYERS — FIXED TEXT

These prayers are spoken at table and must be word-for-word identical across chapters. **Do not paraphrase them when reading aloud.**

**1. *Por la tierra que fue buena*** *(Tomás's prayer — first heard C1, Tomás at the pit's rim)*

> *"Por la tierra que fue buena, y por la tierra que se cansó. Que la tierra descanse, y que nosotros pasemos ligeros."*
>
> *— "For the land that was good, and for the land that grew tired. May the land rest, and may we pass lightly."*

(Reprised in C8 by Don Eusebio in the outer ring; reprised in C12 by Padre Albarrán on the dock before the *Hic manet*.)

**2. Rosa's silent prayer** *(C2, when she lights the candle in the chapel ruin)*

She does not speak this aloud. The text is for the GM's reference only. If pressed by a player, Rosa will say only: *"No es para los oídos. Es para la tierra."* — *"It isn't for ears. It's for the land."*

The unspoken text:

> *"San Ramón Nonato, padrino de los partos imposibles, asistime acá. Que se cierre lo abierto. Que se cubra lo descubierto. Que pase la luz mala sin tocar a esta gente. Y si toca, que toque a la tierra y no a la sangre."*
>
> *— "Saint Raymond Nonnatus, godfather of impossible births, assist me here. May what is open close. May what is uncovered be covered. May the bad light pass without touching these people. And if it touches, may it touch the land and not the blood."*

**3. The Mapudungun counter-chant** *(C3, Kuyen's tribal binding; reprised C8 by all NPCs in the outer ring)*

The chant is not a prayer in the Catholic sense; it is a four-line invocation Kuyen learned from her grandmother. The Mapudungun text and a Spanish gloss:

> *"Mapu küme felen, mapu küme felen, / wenu mapu, kalku mapu, / kuyfi che, kuyfi che, / amul-amulkiawn küme tripayam."*
>
> *— "Earth be well, earth be well, / above-earth, below-earth, / old people, old people, / walking-walking, may the good come out."*

(The Mapudungun is intentionally simplified — it is what a 30-year-old woman in 1821 would have learned from a grandmother born around 1750. It is not authoritative liturgical Mapudungun. **The point at the table is rhythm and sincerity, not linguistic accuracy.**)

## 10. AUDIT WORKFLOW — HOW TO USE THIS DOCUMENT

When auditing or writing a new read-aloud:

1. **Identify the speaker / register.** Rural gaucho, urban porteño, indigenous? Pick from §3–§5.
2. **Pick voseo or usted.** Use the matrix in §2.
3. **Run the anachronism scan.** Cross-check against §6.
4. **Use the in-fiction glossary.** Don't substitute synonyms for the campaign terms in §7.
5. **Quote the named prayers verbatim.** No paraphrase. See §8.
6. **Read aloud at a measured pace.** 1820s Spanish does not have the cadence of 21st-century Buenos Aires speech. Slow it down. Drop the *che* almost entirely. Speak from the chest.

Use this when polishing a chapter:
