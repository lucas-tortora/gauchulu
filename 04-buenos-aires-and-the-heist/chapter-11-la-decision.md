# CHAPTER 11 — LA DECISIÓN
### *The Decision*

**Chapter Goal**: Force the move. Show ordinary containment failing. Expose the second compromised Keeper. Get the Cursed Gaucho's terms on the table. End the night with a transport plan, El Patrón aware of it, and the players knowing they are walking into his trap willingly.
**Emotional Arc**: Grief → Investigation → Cold Resolve
**Key Seed**: Every option is wrong. The right move is the least wrong one, and you make it knowing he is waiting at the end of it.

---

## OPENING SITUATION

Saráchaga has been dead twelve hours. The Keepers convene at Mercedes' insistence at the meeting house, in the same room where the body was. The body has been moved to San Ignacio for a private vigil. Galíndez has begun walking the city differently — armed openly, taking different streets to and from anywhere.

In the courtyard well, a brass disc is *still hot*. Padre Albarrán has been at the well since first light, praying, and has not yet had his answer. He will, today. The day will give it to him.

This chapter is about three things: the **investigation of Saráchaga's death** (which leads to the second compromised Keeper), **the operational planning of the transport**, and **the moral weight of the decision**. Run them in parallel — the players will probably split, with one group going to the docks with Galíndez to look at ships, another to the University to inspect the vault directly, another to chase Saráchaga's last hours.

By dusk, there is a vote. By midnight, the plan is on paper. Before dawn, El Patrón will know the plan — because Ana Bermúdez has, by then, been confronted, and the question of whether she is a leak or a traitor will have been answered by what she does in the next twelve hours.

## SCENE 1 — THE VAULT, FINALLY

Mercedes takes the players to the vault at mid-morning. Galíndez and two of his men come along. Albarrán remains at the well; he will not enter the vault today. *"No estoy listo. Lo voy a saber."* — *"I am not ready. I will know."*

### Setting Description

> *The locked stairwell behind the door marked* Archivo Privado *opens into a corridor of unfinished brick that slopes down. The slope is gentle for the first ten meters, then steepens. The air changes — not damp, drier than the cloister above, with the same mineral edge you remember from the burial pit and from La Salamanca. The walls are blank for the first hundred meters, then begin to carry small marks scratched at chest height: tally lines in clusters of seven. Mercedes counts them under her breath as you walk.*
>
> *"Trescientos cuarenta y cuatro,"* she says when you reach the bottom. *"Esa es la cantidad de Custodios desde 1610. Cada uno hace una marca cuando entra por primera vez."* — *"Three hundred and forty-four. That is the number of Keepers since 1610. Each one makes a mark when they enter for the first time."*
>
> *The corridor ends in a circular room four meters across. The ceiling is low. The walls are limestone — the same imported limestone as the carving in the Jesuit ruins from Arc 2, brought from somewhere none of the local quarries match. In the center of the room stands a chest. Iron-bound, plain, unornamented. Three locks. Two of the locks are old. One is new — installed in 1810.*

> **Keeper-context (B7) — two different dates, do not confuse them at the table**:
> - **1610**: the year the *vault* was sealed. The room itself, the limestone walls, the carved circle on the floor, the binding of *the place*. Pedro de Mendoza's second-generation administrators. This is the geometry that contains both book and El Patrón.
> - **1810**: the year a *third lock* was added to the chest inside the vault. Not by the Keepers. By a private hand, with a private maker's mark, in the same year the *cabildo abierto* convened upstairs. The chest was *not opened* in 1810 — the lock was *added*, which means someone was given access to the chest long enough to install hardware on it. That access is what Mercedes did not authorize. **The lock is the question, not the year.** When a player asks *"What about the 1810 thing?"*, the GM should always check which 1810 they mean: the *cabildo abierto* (political), the Vatican-frigate transfer attempt (logistics), or the lock (security). They are three separate threads in the same year.
>
> *On the floor around the chest, in a circle five meters across — wider than the room, extending under the walls — is the binding geometry. The same shape. The complete dual diagram. Carved into the stone, then recut, then recut again. The grooves have been deepened by hands holding chisels for centuries.*

> **Visual handout**: the same `assets/dual-diagram.svg` the players saw at the Jesuit ruins in C5. Three hundred and forty-four Keepers have walked the line of this circle. The chest sits at the *anchor* (where Saens lay, where Nahuel knelt, where the Marked PC may stand at the dock in C12). The continuity should be unmistakable when you place the handout on the table.

**Mechanical effects of being in the room**:

- The Marked make a 2d6 roll. Snake eyes here would be catastrophic — there is nowhere for a Hound to *not* manifest in a four-meter circular room. Treat double 1 as a near-miss: the Mark heats the chamber by ten degrees, the candles all gutter, the iron of the chest creaks, but nothing manifests. The geometry holds. The players feel it hold around them.
- A **Hard Cthulhu Mythos** roll on the chest's locks: the new lock (1810) is *not* a Keeper lock. The keyway is wrong; the maker's mark is European, but a private maker's mark, the kind put on commissions for very specific clients. The lock was added by someone who either (a) thought the existing security was inadequate, or (b) wanted a key in their own pocket. **Mercedes did not put this lock on**. She is visibly disturbed when she sees it.

> **C11 redundancy (clue retrieval — primary signal)**: The Hard Cthulhu Mythos roll is the *forensic* read on the lock. The *primary* signal at the table is **Mercedes's visible reaction**, which is automatic and requires no roll. She kneels at the chest, looks at the third lock for three full seconds, and goes pale. Her hand goes to her throat. She says — without looking up — *"Esa cerradura no la pusimos nosotros."* — *"We did not put that lock there."* Then, more quietly: *"Y no la pusimos en mi vida."* — *"And we did not put it there in my lifetime."* Mercedes has been a senior Keeper for thirty-eight years and knows every Keeper-installed lock in this vault by sight. **The wrong-lock signal is delivered by her face before any roll is made.** Players who go on to make the Hard Cthulhu Mythos roll get the *maker's mark* detail (private European commission), which sets up Lacasa-via-uncle-Tiburcio in the C11 cloister scene. Players who do not still know, from Mercedes alone, that someone with no business being there has been in this room.
- A **Spot Hidden** on the floor near the chest: fresh dust patterns. *Someone has been in this room within the last two weeks*. Mercedes has not. Albarrán has not. Galíndez has not. Saráchaga had not (per the dust — he came down once a year, in November). *Someone has been visiting the chest.*

> **C10 redundancy (clue retrieval)**: If no player makes the Spot Hidden, **Mercedes herself notices** as she steps around the chest to read the locks. She crouches, runs her thumb across the floor, and stops. *"Hay polvo nuevo. Alguien estuvo acá hace menos de dos semanas. No fui yo. No fue Eladio. No fue Ramón. No fue Galíndez."* — *"There is fresh dust. Someone was here less than two weeks ago. It was not me. It was not Eladio. It was not Ramón. It was not Galíndez."* Mercedes naming this herself ensures the clue lands at the table; the players inherit her conclusion without needing the roll. (A successful Spot Hidden by a player still earns the *additional* detail that the dust pattern shows two distinct sets of footprints — the visitor came twice, three days apart. Mercedes does not see this herself.)

**SAN Check**: 1/1D4 — proximity to the book itself, which the players cannot see but can feel. The chest does not radiate. It absorbs. It is a hole the size of a chest, in the shape of a chest, in the world.

### What Mercedes Does Not Open

She does *not* open the chest. *"Lo abrimos para moverlo. No para mostrarlo."* — *"We open it to move it. Not to show it."* She is right. The players accept this or they do not, but if they push to open it, Mercedes refuses and Galíndez physically interposes himself. The book stays unseen until the transport.

### "If they have the key, why haven't they taken it?"

A natural player question, the moment they realize El Patrón (or his proxy) has been visiting the chest. Mercedes answers — quietly, with one hand flat on the lid:

> *"El candado lo puede abrir él. La bóveda no lo deja entrar. Toda esta sala — el círculo en el piso, los trescientos cuarenta y cuatro Custodios, la piedra que vino del norte — es un dibujo, y el dibujo lo contiene a él tanto como contiene al libro. Mientras el libro esté abajo, dentro del dibujo, él puede tocar el cofre pero no puede llevárselo. El libro saliendo, atravesando la calle, es lo único que le permite completar el dibujo afuera y reclamarlo. Por eso necesita que lo movamos nosotros. Por eso lleva cuarenta años empujándonos a moverlo."*

> *"He can open the lock. The vault will not let him enter. This whole room — the circle on the floor, the three hundred and forty-four Keepers, the stone brought from the north — is a drawing, and the drawing contains him as much as it contains the book. While the book is below, inside the drawing, he can touch the chest but cannot take it. The book leaving, crossing the street, is the only thing that lets him complete the drawing outside and claim it. That is why he needs *us* to move it. That is why he has spent forty years pushing us to move it."*

This reframes the entire C11–C12 plan. The transport is not a *security problem* — it is the very condition El Patrón has been engineering. The Keepers are moving the book *anyway* because the alternative (leaving it under a containment that is now warm-going-hot) is worse. They are walking into the trap with eyes open, banking on Kuyen's cordon and Galíndez's fifteen men and the players' Marks to break the geometry *during* the transport.

**Galíndez, after a beat**: *"Entonces estamos jugando su juego, pero con nuestras reglas."* — *"So we are playing his game, but with our rules."*

### What Mercedes Says, Before Leaving the Room

> *"El que abrió el candado de 1810 está vivo. Está en la ciudad. Lo vamos a encontrar antes de la noche."* — *"Whoever opened the 1810 lock is alive. Is in the city. We are going to find them before nightfall."*

This is the chapter's primary investigation thread.

## SCENE 2 — THE DOCKS

Galíndez takes whoever is interested down to the *Ribera Norte* and the new river docks. The Riachuelo to the south has the *saladeros* and the working ships; the Río de la Plata to the east has the deep-water vessels.

### What Galíndez Has Lined Up

- **The *Carmen del Pilar***: a Genoese brig, captained by **Capitán Ettore Borghi**, who has worked with Galíndez before during the British invasions. He is honest, mercenary, and does not ask questions if paid in silver. Ready to sail in 36 hours. Destination: a small chapel on the Banda Oriental (modern-day Uruguay) — a Keeper-allied chapter house in Colonia.
- **A second, decoy vessel**: *La Niña Encantada*, a coastal schooner that will leave at the same hour, carrying a sealed wooden chest of identical exterior — packed with old bricks and one decoy lockbox containing nothing. Decoy captain: bribed, kept in the dark.
- **Galíndez's plan in summary**: Two ships, two routes, the real book on the brig. The chest leaves the vault at midnight, travels the streets in a closed *galera* (a heavy carriage) escorted by his fifteen men, transfers at the Riachuelo dock into a launch, and is rowed to the brig anchored in the river.

### Where Galíndez Is Worried

> *"Hay tres puntos donde no puedo cubrirlos a todos: la salida de la Universidad, la esquina de Belgrano y Defensa donde la galera tiene que doblar despacio, y el embarque al bote en el muelle. Tres puntos. Ahí es donde nos van a pegar."* — *"There are three places I can't cover everyone: the University exit, the corner of Belgrano and Defensa where the carriage has to turn slow, and the transfer to the launch at the dock. Three points. That is where we will be hit."*

**Player input matters here**. They can:
- Recruit additional men (a tribal binding cordon, the Cursed Gaucho's contacts, friends of friends from Inocencia, drunk gauchos at a *pulpería* — Persuade / Credit Rating / Charm rolls for each).
- Suggest alternative routes (Galíndez listens; he will adopt a player route if the player can defend it operationally).
- Insist on accompanying the carriage (Galíndez says *"sí"* but warns: *"Si los matan a ustedes ahí, también pierdo a los marcados, y los marcados son los que pueden ver llegar a la cosa."* — *"If you are killed there, I also lose the Marked, and the Marked are who can see the *thing* coming."*).
- Plan the transfer at the dock differently (a third boat, a feint, etc.).

**Mechanic**: every concrete plan element the players contribute *with reasoning* reduces the Chapter 12 transport difficulty by one step at the relevant moment. Mark these on the GM sheet.

## SCENE 3 — TRACING SARÁCHAGA'S LAST HOURS

Players who pursue the murder investigation get the most dangerous information.

### Step 1: The kitchen

A maid at Saráchaga's house — **Luisa**, 19, terrified — saw a *visitante* arrive at the second-patio entrance at approximately 11 PM. She did not see his face. He was wearing a hat, a dark coat, and *boots that were not city boots*. They were spurred. Frontier.

> *"Eran botas con espuelas, y golpeaban el suelo de un modo que no era de la ciudad. Eran botas que estaban acostumbradas al campo."*  — *"They were spurred boots, and they hit the ground in a way that wasn't a city way. They were boots used to the country."*

Possible interpretations:
- **Quirce** wears city boots. Not him.
- **The Cursed Gaucho** wears spurred boots. He is a candidate — but he has no motive to kill Saráchaga (Saráchaga was the strongest voice for keeping the book where the Cursed Gaucho would have wanted it kept).
- **Don Bartolomé Lacasa** — the absent Keeper from Chapter 10, the one Mercedes did not name. He is from a rural family; he has an estancia north of the city. He wears spurred boots.

### Step 2: The well

The brass disc at the bottom of the well, recovered by lowering a small *vigilante urbano* on a rope, is real. It is no longer hot — it has cooled to warm overnight. **Albarrán** confirms: *"La piedra responde a la presión. La presión bajó. Algo está organizando la presión."* — *"The stone responds to pressure. The pressure went down. Something is organizing the pressure."* (El Patrón eased off after the kill. He wanted Saráchaga dead; once dead, he reduced pressure to avoid forcing the Keepers' hand prematurely. He wants the players to vote, deliberate, and *plan*. He wants the transport.)

### Step 3: Don Bartolomé Lacasa

Lacasa's town house is on Calle Reconquista, three blocks from the meeting house. He is not there. The maid says he left for *"el campo"* yesterday — the country — without warning. **Investigators with Track or Stealth**: Lacasa's spurred boots have left a print at his own back door, in his own dust. He left at *11:45 PM* the night before — fifteen minutes after the *visitante* in spurred boots arrived at Saráchaga's. He did not have time to be at home before then.

Lacasa is the killer. Not by his hand — he was a delivery vehicle. He brought El Patrón to Saráchaga.

### Step 4: Lacasa's office

If the players search his office (forced entry, or via Galíndez who has the relevant letters for a search warrant from a sympathetic *alcalde*): a half-burned letter in the fireplace, addressed in a handwriting the players will recognize from Arc 2 — Quirce's. The letter was a courtesy; Lacasa was not Quirce's man, he was *El Patrón's* directly, and Quirce was simply notifying him.

There is also a deed in Lacasa's strongbox: an estancia north of the city, in *Areco*, registered in the name of "**E. Dadálah**" — an alias El Patrón has used in land transactions before. The deed signature has a sigil drawn over the date.

**SAN Check**: 0/1 — the cumulative recognition that El Patrón has been a *legal property owner in Buenos Aires Province for at least a decade*.

### Step 5: The conclusion

Lacasa rode to his Areco estancia. He is not coming back. By the time the players have this information, El Patrón is already *in* the city, having arrived during the players' investigation. He is not at Lacasa's house. He is somewhere they have not yet looked.

## SCENE 4 — ANA BERMÚDEZ

By late afternoon, the players (or Mercedes, or Galíndez) connect Ana to Quirce — through a slip in her notes from Chapter 10, through a piece of cipher in Lacasa's letter that matches her hand, or through an accidental meeting (she is at the meeting house when the players return).

### The Confrontation

Mercedes confronts her quietly, in the second patio, the players present if they want to be.

> *"Ana. ¿Cuánto te dijo Quirce?"* — *"Ana. How much did Quirce tell you?"*

Ana goes white. She does not deny. She does not run. She sits down at the long pine table — the same table where the meeting was held — and puts her notebook in front of her with both hands flat on it.

> *"Me dijo que estaba estudiando los anillos sismológicos de la cuenca del Riachuelo. Que mis cifras le ayudaban a entender ciertos patrones. Yo le mandaba copias de las actas de las reuniones — pensaba que eran inocuas."*  — *"He told me he was studying seismological rings of the Riachuelo basin. That my ciphers were helping him understand certain patterns. I was sending him copies of the meeting minutes — I thought they were harmless."*

She is not lying. She believed this. She is also — in the moment of saying it — realizing exactly what she did. The realization is in her face.

### What She Knows

- Quirce paid her in books — specifically, two volumes from his private library that she had wanted for years (*Disquisitiones Arithmeticae*, in the original Latin; and a Cardano).
- She has sent him every meeting minute for six months.
- Quirce passed pieces of those minutes to a third party, whom she did not know about until now.
- The third party was Lacasa. Through Lacasa, El Patrón.

### The Decision

Mercedes does not throw her out. *"Vas a ayudar mañana. Vas a estar ahí. Y después de que esto termine, vamos a hablar de qué eras."*  — *"You are going to help tomorrow. You are going to be there. And after this is over, we will talk about what you were."* This is mercy and a knife at the same time.

**If players intervene**: Ana asks for the chance to send one final piece of information to Quirce — a *false* piece, prepared by the players or Galíndez. This is a chance to feed the enemy bad intel. **Players who design the false intel well** (route changes, fake timeline, decoy specifics that match Galíndez's plan) gain Chapter 12 advantage: El Patrón's first attack hits the wrong location.

**If players exclude her**: Ana is held under guard at the meeting house through C12. The fake-intel option is lost. Mercedes is colder for the rest of the campaign.

### Padre Albarrán Arrives

While Ana is at the table, Albarrán comes in from the third patio. He is wet — he has been near the well, possibly in the well. He looks at the players, at Mercedes, at Ana. He says one sentence.

> *"Dios me contestó. Lo movemos."* — *"God answered me. We move it."*

He sits down. Galíndez nods once. The vote is over before it is taken.

## SCENE 5 — THE CURSED GAUCHO

Mid-evening, a runner arrives from the south. The Cursed Gaucho is at the tannery on the Riachuelo. He will not come to the meeting house. He wants the players, alone, before midnight.

### The Tannery

> *The smell hits before the building does — a sour-sweet rot of hides curing in lime and quebracho bark. The Riachuelo is twenty meters away, brown and slow. The tannery itself is a low brick building with three slave-built drying sheds behind it. The Cursed Gaucho is in the third shed, the one farthest from the road, sitting on a tanning bench beside three of his men and two — possibly three — of the surviving tribal allies the players brought to the city. The tribal allies are eating from a shared bowl. The Cursed Gaucho is sharpening a knife.*

### The Gaucho's Position

He has heard about Saráchaga. He has heard about the well-stone. He has heard about the planned move. He has been preparing, all afternoon, for what he intends to do tomorrow.

> *"Mañana yo voy en la galera con ustedes. No voy a llegar al barco. Mi pelea es en la calle, no en el agua."* — *"Tomorrow I go in the carriage with you. I am not going to reach the ship. My fight is in the street, not on the water."*

This is his commitment. He has chosen the place of his death. He is asking the players to *let him die there*. He is also asking for one specific promise.

### His Terms

> *"Si lo ven a él — el que tiene el campo en Areco con el otro nombre — no me dejen darle a alguien más mi pelea. Ese hombre es mío. Si no me dejan llegar a él yo, no me hacen el favor que vine a pedir."*  — *"If you see him — the one with the field in Areco under the other name — don't let me give my fight away to someone else. That man is mine. If you don't let me reach him myself, you are not doing the favor I came to ask."*

He is asking the players to not kill El Patrón before he can reach him. He is also asking them to not be in his way when the moment comes. This is a real promise. It will cost.

**If the players agree**: The Cursed Gaucho stands. He takes from his belt a *cuchillo de campo* with a wooden handle worn smooth, and lays it on the bench in front of them. *"Si yo no llego, este es para él. Hagan lo que tengan que hacer con esto, pero llévenlo a él."* — *"If I don't make it, this is for him. Do what you need to do with it, but bring it to him."* The knife is now an item the players carry into C12. It is not magical. It is the Cursed Gaucho's family's, three generations old, used in the work of the *campo*. He has been wearing it, today, for the first time in years.

**If the players refuse**: He says nothing. He goes back to sharpening. He does not show up at the carriage. He hits El Patrón his own way, alone, in the streets — and he dies the same way, but the players are deprived of his fifteen minutes of cover. Mark the difference for C12.

### The Tribal Allies at the Tannery

They have been here since Chapter 10's missing-ally moment. The Cursed Gaucho did not abduct them. They came willingly because he was the only person in the city offering them a binding ritual to perform during the transport. **Kuyen** (if alive) explains:

> *"Las calles son la geometría. Si el libro va por las calles, alguien tiene que ir delante con un cordón. Yo voy. Hay que."* — *"The streets are the geometry. If the book is going through the streets, someone has to go ahead with a binding cordon. I go. It must be."*

She has been preparing the ritual all day. It is similar to the Arc 1 binding but at city scale — leather panels held by hand, drawn at sequential corners along the route, activated by her presence and Nahuel's (if alive) and the surviving Wrong Returned's (if Marta or Héctor lived through Arc 3). The ritual will create a moving cordon ten meters around the carriage that El Patrón's surface manifestations cannot easily cross.

**This is the players' single largest advantage in Chapter 12**, and it depends on their having brought tribal allies through to Buenos Aires alive.

## SCENE 6 — DOÑA MERCEDES, ALONE

Late evening. The plan is set: the move is at midnight tomorrow. The vote was done. Galíndez is at the docks finalizing. Albarrán is back at the well. Ana is writing the false intel under guard.

Mercedes asks one player — by name, the one she has spoken to most directly — to walk with her in the cloister of San Ignacio. The cloister is empty at this hour. The bell tower is dark.

She tells them what she has been carrying since Arc 2.

> *"Cuando yo era aprendiza, hace cuarenta años, copié una sección del manuscrito de La Salamanca al original que está en la bóveda. No era una sección decorativa. Era una sección del Necronomicón. La copié por curiosidad. Por orgullo. Pensé que la podía estudiar y devolver."*
>
> *"I am about to be useful in a way that is not completely clean. The reason El Patrón has been hunting me for thirty years is not because I am a Keeper. It is because I, when I was twenty-four, copied a section of the Necronomicón from the original — for personal study — and that section traveled, in my hand, into the Salamanca manuscripts that the cult later read. The reason the Great Wizard had partial knowledge is in part my fault. The fact that the cult could attempt the awakening at all — partly my fault. I have been atoning, in the position of teacher, for forty years. I have not been forgiven. I do not expect to be."*

If asked **how** the copy traveled — which a player will, if they are paying attention — Mercedes answers without hesitation. She has rehearsed this answer for forty years.

> *"Cuando dejé el aprendizaje, le entregué mi cuaderno al archivo de los Custodios. Pensé que ahí estaba seguro. Lo estaba — durante seis meses. Después alguien lo abrió y copió las páginas de mi mano. No lo quemaron, lo copiaron. Yo no me di cuenta hasta dos años más tarde, cuando una de mis frases volvió a aparecer en una carta que un cura de Salta le mandó a un inquisidor en Lima. Era mi puntuación. Era mi forma de poner las comas."*
>
> *"When I left the apprenticeship, I gave my notebook to the Keepers' archive. I thought it was safe there. It was — for six months. Then someone opened it and copied the pages from my hand. They didn't burn it, they copied it. I didn't realize until two years later, when one of my own sentences came back in a letter from a priest in Salta to an inquisitor in Lima. It was my punctuation. It was the way I place commas."*

She names the suspected agent only when the player presses:

> *"El predecesor de Lacasa. Tiburcio Lacasa, su tío. Murió en 1812. Era Custodio entonces. No tengo prueba — y por eso, durante cuarenta años, no podía señalarlo. Pero el cuaderno estuvo en su cuidado durante esos seis meses. Y la familia Lacasa siempre tuvo, digamos, hábitos de archivo flexibles."*
>
> *"Lacasa's predecessor. Tiburcio Lacasa, his uncle. Died in 1812. He was a Keeper at the time. I have no proof — which is why, for forty years, I could not name him. But the notebook was in his care during those six months. And the Lacasa family has always had, let us say, flexible archival habits."*

This connects the dots from Mercedes' apprenticeship → the archive → the Lacasa family → the Salamanca cult's reading material → Don Bartolomé Lacasa's present-day betrayal. *The chain is forty years long and runs through one family.* Players who note this can extrapolate that El Patrón has been cultivating the Lacasa line as long as he has been hunting Mercedes.

This is her confession. **SAN Check**: 0/1 — not horror, the hard kind of intimacy.

### What She Asks

> *"No te pido que perdones. Te pido que cuando ese hombre nos diga mañana de noche que yo soy la que abrió la puerta, ustedes ya lo sepan por mí. No por él."*  — *"I am not asking you to forgive. I am asking you, when that man tells us tomorrow night that I am the one who opened the door, that you already know it from me. Not from him."*

She is preparing the players for El Patrón's reveal in C12 — he *will* try to weaponize Mercedes' history against her. She wants them inoculated.

### What She Offers

She gives the player a small object: a folded square of parchment, sealed in wax with a crude figure — the same shape as the binding in Arc 1, but inside-out.

> *"Si en la noche de mañana llega un momento en que tienen que decidir entre esconder, usar, o destruir — y tienen que destruir — ábranlo. Es de mi mano. Está en lo que yo copié. Es la única forma que conozco de cerrar el libro entero, no solo la fuga."*  — *"If tomorrow night a moment comes when you have to decide between hiding, using, or destroying — and you have to destroy — open it. It is in my hand. It is from what I copied. It is the only way I know of closing the entire book, not just the leak."*

She does not say what destroying does to the destroyer. She does not need to. The Marked players know.

The folded parchment is now an item the players carry into C12. **Do not open it tonight. Mercedes specifically asks them not to.**

> **What is on the parchment (Keeper-only — players do not learn this until C12)**: The folded square is *two* pages of Mercedes's hand-copied work, joined at the spine and folded together. The outer page is the **destroy-ritual** Mercedes describes above — the older Tehuelche-Tobá-Salamanca chain, condensed to the form Mercedes copied from a section the cult-side scholars never reached. The inner page, which a player only sees if they unfold the parchment fully and turn it over, is the **binding section** of the Necronomicón itself — the *use-ritual*. Mercedes copied it forty years ago in the same week she copied the destroy-ritual. She has never used it. She has never shown it to anyone. **It is the only existing copy outside the book in the vault.** A player who unfolds the parchment all the way at the table tonight will see it; Mercedes asked them not to, but if they do, she will look at them quietly and say only: *"Si lo viste, lo viste. Mañana decidís qué hacer con eso."* — *"If you saw it, you saw it. Tomorrow you decide what to do with that."*

### What Mercedes Says to the Reader-PC, If the Reader-Path Was Earned

*(Only run this beat if the C5 page-79 seed was planted and the C8 Mercedes-recognition exchange happened. Otherwise skip; the inner page of the parchment will be the only foreshadowing of Use in C12, and most players will not unfold it.)*

After the parchment changes hands, Mercedes turns to the PC who read Saens aloud — the same PC she identified at the camp in C8 — and speaks to that PC alone, while the others are still looking at the parchment:

> *"Vos sabés qué dije recién sobre la página de adentro. Si mañana llega ese momento — el de leer, no el de destruir — abrís el papel del lado que no te dije, y lees vos. Yo no lo puedo hacer. Yo lo copié hace cuarenta años; eso fue lo que me costó la apprenticeship, eso fue lo que me hizo Custodia, eso es lo que me hace incapaz de usarlo. La copia me salió perfecta porque no entendí lo que estaba copiando. Si mañana lo entendés y lo lees — vas a quedar como yo, pero al revés: vas a entender, y no vas a poder copiar nunca más."*
>
> *"You know what I just said about the inner page. If tomorrow that moment comes — the moment of reading, not of destroying — you unfold the paper from the side I did not tell you about, and you read. I cannot do it. I copied it forty years ago; that was the price of my apprenticeship, that was what made me a Keeper, that is what makes me incapable of using it. The copy came out perfect because I did not understand what I was copying. If tomorrow you understand and read it — you will end like me, but in reverse: you will understand, and you will never be able to copy again."*

This is the **Use offer made explicit**, in private, to the one PC who earned it. The Reader-PC now knows three things:
1. The Use ending is real and on the table.
2. They are the one who can take it.
3. The cost is not just SAN — it is a permanent inability to ever again be on the *teaching* side of any geometric tradition. They will *know*, but they will not be able to *transmit*. They will be a Mercedes-in-reverse: a one-way receiver.

Mercedes does not ask the PC to commit. She simply tells them. **The PC may share this with the table at any point or never share it.** A PC who keeps the offer secret and chooses Use at the dock is making the campaign's heaviest one-player choice. A PC who shares the offer and lets the table debate is making a different, equally valid choice. Both are correct.

### What She Tells the Player About Albarrán

Before they leave the cloister, the player asks — or Mercedes volunteers; she will not let this go unsaid:

> *"Mañana Ramón Albarrán va a venir al muelle. No fue su plan original. Era custodiar las esquinas durante el transporte. Pero si llegamos al muelle y la geometría se cierra alrededor del libro y de él, Ramón sabe la fórmula latina. La aprendió de Saráchaga en 1798. La fórmula no es magia. Es un intercambio."*
>
> *"Tomorrow Ramón Albarrán will come to the dock. That was not his original plan — he was to consecrate the corners during the transport. But if we reach the dock and the geometry closes around the book and around him, Ramón knows the Latin formula. He learned it from Saráchaga in 1798. The formula is not magic. It is a trade."*

She lets the silence hold. The player asks. Mercedes answers:

> *"El Hic manet ata el lugar al tiempo que le queda al hombre que lo dice. Saráchaga lo iba a decir él, después de los cuarenta años de oposición — por eso lo mataron antes de que pudiera. Ramón está en condición de decirlo ahora. No le voy a pedir que lo haga. No me corresponde. Esa es una decisión que toma él, mañana, cuando vea lo que vea."*
>
> *"The 'Hic manet' binds the place to the time the man speaking it has left. Saráchaga was going to say it himself, after forty years of opposition — that is why he was killed before he could. Ramón is in a position to say it now. I am not going to ask him to. That is not mine to ask. That is a decision he makes tomorrow, when he sees what he sees."*

This is the foreshadowing. The Hide ending is now a *choice with a known cost*, and the player carrying this conversation into C12 knows that asking Albarrán to speak the Latin is asking him to die. **The player does not have to share this with the rest of the table** — Mercedes told one player, in a cloister, alone. If they keep it to themselves and Hide is chosen anyway, that is one player's particular weight.

> **Reconciliation note (B6, Keeper)**: Albarrán's *"Dios me contestó. Lo movemos."* in Scene 4 means *move the book to the dock and re-vault it under fresh consecration*. It does not mean *put it on a ship*. By morning, when the *Hic manet* option crystallizes for him at the dock, he is not reversing — he is finishing the same answer. The players who only heard *"Lo movemos"* will read C12 as a reversal; the player who heard the cloister conversation can correct that read at the table when the moment lands.

## SCENE 7 — MIDNIGHT, BACK AT INOCENCIA'S

The players return to the boarding house around midnight. Inocencia has been waiting up. There is bread on the table. There is also a *visitor*.

In the parlor, sitting in Inocencia's chair, is a man the players have never seen and yet they have met him in a hundred small forms across four arcs.

He is dressed simply. He is not large. His face is unremarkable in a way that should be impossible — a face that the eye keeps trying to find a feature to remember and fails. He smiles. He stands. He bows slightly.

> *"Qué bueno conocernos al fin. Soy Don Eligio Dadálah. Ustedes me conocen como El Patrón."*  — *"How good to meet you at last. I am Don Eligio Dadálah. You know me as El Patrón."*

He is alone. He is unarmed. He has come to Inocencia's parlor at midnight, the night before the transport, *to talk*.

**This is the chapter's final scene**.

### What He Wants

He wants to negotiate. He wants to say, in his own voice, what he has been saying through proxies for the entire campaign. He wants to make the offer.

> *"No me quieren matar. No tendrían tiempo. Y yo no quiero matarlos. Lo que quiero es que mañana hagan exactamente lo que están planeando hacer. Mover el libro. Llevarlo por la calle. Dárselo, sin saberlo, a la geometría que vine yo a despertar. Hagan eso, y al final de mañana yo los dejo vivir. Si no lo hacen — si lo dejan abajo — voy a tener que romper la bóveda yo mismo, y eso va a costarles mucho más que un viaje de carruaje."*
>
> *"You don't want to kill me. You wouldn't have time. And I don't want to kill you. What I want is for you tomorrow to do exactly what you are planning to do. Move the book. Take it through the street. Give it, without knowing, to the geometry I came to wake. Do that, and at the end of tomorrow I let you live. If you don't — if you leave it below — I will have to break the vault myself, and that will cost you much more than a carriage ride."*

He is telling the truth, mostly. He *will* let them live if the move happens. He *will* break the vault if they refuse. The choice is real.

**The trap**: he is not telling them that the transport route is the one he wants. He needs the book *moving*, not stationary, because of the way his geometry interacts with the city grid. Moving the book is what allows him to *take* it.

### What He Will Not Do

He will not threaten Mercedes in the parlor. He will not name Lacasa. He will not promise specifics. He is a man closing a deal — and like all good closers, he leaves the customer with the impression that everything is reasonable.

### What He Says, Last, Before Leaving

He turns at the door and says, to whichever player he has been addressing most:

> *"Doña Mercedes les contó esta noche lo que copió hace cuarenta años. Tomó coraje. La quiero, ¿saben? Es de las pocas personas que me han hecho trabajar. Cuando mañana terminemos, voy a pedirles un favor: déjenmela viva. Yo la voy a tratar bien."*
>
> *"Doña Mercedes told you tonight what she copied forty years ago. That took courage. I love her, you know? She is one of the few people who has made me work. When we finish tomorrow, I will ask you a favor: leave her alive for me. I will treat her well."*

He bows again. He leaves. The door closes on its own.

**SAN Check**: 1/1D6 — a single conversation with the avatar of Nyarlathotep, in a boarding-house parlor, with bread on the table.

### After He Is Gone

Inocencia is asleep at the kitchen table, head on her arms — she has been asleep since before midnight, and no one heard him come in. She wakes with a small gasp when the door closes. She does not remember him.

In the parlor: a single thin object on the chair where he sat. A pencil. A new one. Recently sharpened. Not threatening. A gift, or a joke, or a calling card. The players will carry the pencil. Nothing they do with it produces any effect. It is just a pencil.

## END OF CHAPTER 11

The chapter ends in Inocencia's parlor at 12:30 AM. Bread on the table. Pencil on the chair. The plan in the players' heads. The Cursed Gaucho's knife on one belt. Mercedes' folded parchment in another pocket. Saráchaga's notebook on a third.

**Mercedes**, summoned from the meeting house, arrives at 1 AM and listens to the players describe what was said. She does not interrupt. When they finish, she says:

> *"Bueno. Mañana hacemos lo que planeamos. Y cuando llegue el momento, decidimos si dejarlo vivo a él, no a mí."*  — *"Good. Tomorrow we do what we planned. And when the moment comes, we decide whether to let *him* live, not me."*

**End image (read aloud)**:

> *The pencil is on the table beside the bread. Mercedes is at the window. Galíndez has already gone — there is no time for sleep tonight. Outside, on Calle Belgrano, a clock somewhere in the city begins to strike one. It strikes once. The single bell rings, and rings, and rings, and does not stop ringing for ten seconds longer than a single bell should ring. Then it stops. The street is silent.*
>
> *In your room, on the bedside table, you find a single object you do not remember placing there: the small brass disc of a witness-stone. It is hot.*

## GM NOTES

**This chapter is the moral center of the arc**: The players will be moved by Mercedes' confession, by Albarrán's quiet *"Lo movemos,"* by Ana's face when she realizes what she did, by the Cursed Gaucho's knife. Don't rush any of these moments. They are the campaign's emotional payload.

**El Patrón in the parlor is the boldest scene in the campaign**: Run it slowly. He is *not* dramatic. He is courteous. He is the most dangerous person any of these players will ever meet. The horror is in the manners. He is reasonable, the offer is real, and *some players will be tempted by it*. That is correct.

**The pencil**: Don't explain the pencil. The pencil is not Chekhov's gun. The pencil is that El Patrón's small kindnesses are more frightening than his violence.

**Lacasa is off stage**: He flees to Areco. The players cannot reach him before C12. If they pursue, they find his estancia abandoned, the deed papers gone, his servants gone north. Save Lacasa for after the campaign as a hook (he was a footman; El Patrón doesn't keep him).

**The witness-stone in the bedroom**: Hot. The vault is in active failure. The players have until midnight tomorrow before something happens regardless of plan.

**Track player commitments**:
- Did they agree to the Cursed Gaucho's terms? (Affects C12 Act 2.)
- Did they let Ana send false intel? (Affects C12 Act 1.)
- Did they help recruit additional men for Galíndez? (Affects each transport stage.)
- Are tribal allies still alive and willing to walk the cordon? (Affects C12 Act 1 directly — the cordon is the players' single biggest mechanical edge.)
- Do they have Mercedes' folded parchment? (One of the three C12 endings depends on it.)
- Do they have Saráchaga's notebook? (Lets them recognize Lacasa-style names if El Patrón uses them in C12.)
- Do they have the Cursed Gaucho's knife? (Becomes the destroy-by-blade physical instrument if Cursed Gaucho dies before reaching El Patrón.)
- The pencil: in someone's pocket. No effect, but it's there.
