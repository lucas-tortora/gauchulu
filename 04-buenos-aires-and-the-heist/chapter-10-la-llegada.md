## OPENING SITUATION

The players arrive in Buenos Aires from the Andean leg of Arc 3. They are dirty, armed, and visibly frontier — they will be stared at, refused service, charged double. **Doña Mercedes** is with them; the **Cursed Gaucho** rides parallel and refuses any inn (he sleeps in the stables of a friend's tannery near the Riachuelo and sends word how to find him). Surviving tribal allies (Kuyen, Nahuel, Héctor, Marta — depending on Arc 3 outcomes) accompany the party but are conspicuously out of place. Buenos Aires of 1821 does not know what to do with them, and that visibility is itself a danger.

The University of Buenos Aires has existed for one year — it was founded by the Rivadavia government in August 1821 as a secular reorganization of the colonial institutions of higher learning. Its public face is reform: science, law, modern medicine. Its private face — known to perhaps thirty people in the city — is that the **Manzana de las Luces** (the old Jesuit complex of San Ignacio, the Colegio, the *procuraduría*) sits atop a sealed vault that has been there since 1610, when Pedro de Mendoza's second-generation administrators ordered it walled in. Inside the vault: a book.

This chapter's center of gravity is the **Keeper meeting house** in the Barrio del Alto — a private residence belonging to Don Eladio Saráchaga, where the Keepers convene off the books. Doña Mercedes will bring the players there on the evening of the day they arrive. By the morning of the next day, one of the Keepers will be dead.

**What the Keepers know about the players**: Doña Mercedes has been writing. They know the players broke the binding in Arc 1, accidentally summoned a Hound in the same act, that they have Marks, that they survived the Salamanca / closing in the Andes (per Arc 3), and that they are the only outside party with direct experience of El Patrón's reach. Some Keepers consider this an asset. At least one considers it disqualifying.

**What the players don't know yet**: That at least two members of the Keeper inner circle are already, quietly, in El Patrón's pocket. Names are kept secret in this chapter and surfaced through investigation in C11.

## ENTRY INTO THE CITY

### Setting Description

> *The pampa stops, and then the city begins, and then there is nothing in between. The road from the west narrows into Calle de la Recoleta. The first thing you smell is leather curing — the *saladeros* by the river have been running all month — and beneath that, the river itself, brackish and slow. The Riachuelo to the south is a brown line. The Río de la Plata to the east is too wide to see across; the horizon is water without a far shore.*
>
> *Buenos Aires in 1821 is a colonial port half-becoming a republican capital. The streets are still the original Spanish grid, eleven blocks by nine, laid out in 1580. But on every third corner there is a new sign — a bookshop, a French pharmacist, a notary's office where there used to be a confessor's. Rivadavia has been busy. The Cabildo's tower clock keeps city time. Church bells argue with it.*
>
> *And under all of it, in the rhythm of the streets, you feel something you have felt before. The grid is too clean. The angles repeat. Your Marks — quiet for the Andes — wake up the moment you cross into the eleven-by-nine.*

**Tone cues**: Heat off cobblestones, river smells, Castilian Spanish layered over Portuguese over the languages of slaves on the docks. The city is hot, crowded, and watching.

**Mechanical effect on entry**: Any Marked investigator (from Arc 1) makes a 2d6 roll on entering the grid. A double 1 does *not* manifest a Hound — too many people, too much background noise — but it *does* communicate clearly: *the city is itself the geometry.* The Mark is reading the street layout the way it read the burial pit. Pass this information silently to the player.

## SCENE 1 — THE STREETS

### What the City Does to the Party

This is a full scene, not a montage. The players are spending two to three hours just *getting* anywhere. The point is to build the city as a character before introducing the Keepers.

**Possible micro-encounters** (run two or three):

1. **The customs officer at the gate.** A junior official, polite, lazy, demands papers. The party has none in order. A small bribe, a name-drop (Doña Mercedes provides one), or a Persuade / Credit Rating roll resolves it. He logs the names anyway. *Someone* will read that log within twelve hours — it's how one of the compromised Keepers learns the players are in town.

2. **The friend of a friend.** Someone Doña Mercedes hands the party off to: **Inocencia Vallejos**, a widow who runs a small boarding house on Calle Belgrano. She gives them rooms at half-price, asks no questions, and — crucially — knows Doña Mercedes from before her time at the University. *"De cuando estabas en el colegio,"* she says. (Foreshadow: Mercedes was in the Keepers' apprentice cohort. She left. Then she came back.)

3. **The street vendor who flinches.** A *mate* seller on a corner the party crosses several times that day. On the third crossing, an investigator notices: he flinches whenever the Marked investigator passes. **Spot Hidden / Psychology**: he is not afraid of the investigator's appearance. He is afraid of where their *body sits in space* relative to the corner. He has seen this happen before. He won't say more. He's been on this corner for twenty years. He's seen who else has it.

4. **The carriage.** A black carriage, well-made, drawn by two grays, passes the party at the corner of Florida and San Martín. The shutters are drawn. The driver does not look at the party but tips his hat — *to* them, deliberately. **Spot Hidden** on the crest on the door: a stylized angle, three lines forming a partial geometry. If anyone asks Doña Mercedes about the carriage, her face goes still. *"No lo miren directo."* — *"Don't look at it directly."* She does not say whose carriage it is. She knows.

> **C8 redundancy (clue retrieval — the name)**: As the carriage passes, the *mate* seller from beat 3 is on his corner. He looks at the carriage, then at the ground, then mutters under his breath — barely audible, **Listen** roll at Regular difficulty (or just *given* to whichever investigator is closest, no roll, if the pace is dragging): *"…Dadálah…"* That is the only word. He spits. He looks at his hands. He keeps making *mate*. **No further information from him under any circumstances** — he will not repeat the word, will not confirm it, and will be on a different corner the next morning. But the players have heard the name *Dadálah* in C6 (through Quirce's mediumship) and again in C8 (Mercedes's note about the cult naming Dadálah out loud). Now they hear it again, on the street, attached to the carriage. **Three independent sources, no NPC standing in front of them and explaining the connection — they connect it themselves.** This is the Mark's job in city scale: pattern recognition that the players, not Mercedes, are the ones to make.

**SAN Check**: 0/1 — for the Marked, the cumulative recognition of city-as-geometry. For the unmarked, just the disorientation of arrival.

## SCENE 2 — THE UNIVERSITY (PUBLIC FACE)

### Setting Description

> *The Universidad de Buenos Aires occupies the Manzana de las Luces — the old Jesuit block at San Ignacio, between Bolívar, Alsina, Perú, and Moreno. The Jesuit church of San Ignacio still stands on the corner; its bell tower is the oldest visible structure in the city. The Colegio de San Carlos has been renamed the Colegio de Ciencias Morales and reorganized under the new university. Workers are still putting up new lecture halls. Half the rooms smell of fresh whitewash. The other half smell of two centuries of incense and old paper.*
>
> *Doña Mercedes leads you through the cloister of San Ignacio without slowing. Students avoid making eye contact with her. Two of the older priests do not.*

**Keeper Notes — what the players see in this visit**:

- **The library, public room.** Long tables, dictionaries, a partial set of the *Encyclopédie* in French (illegal until last year, now displayed openly). Bored students. A bored librarian — **Mateo Quiroga**, no relation to Rosa from Arc 1; small joke, save it. He is not a Keeper but Mercedes greets him by name.
- **The locked stairwell.** In a corner of the Jesuit complex, behind a door marked *Archivo Privado — No Pasar*. Two locks, modern. No visible guard. Mercedes does not stop here. She glances at it once and keeps moving. **Spot Hidden** while crossing: the doorframe has small pencil marks on it — measurements taken at irregular intervals, somebody is tracking *something* about the door. (This is Quirce's second visit, last week. Mercedes does not know about the marks yet. The players catch it before she does.)
- **The chapel of San Ignacio.** Mercedes goes in alone for fifteen minutes. She prays in front of the altar. Players watching from the doorway: she is also looking at the floor — at the geometric inlays in the marble — and counting under her breath. When she comes out: *"Eso fue para confirmar algo. Vamos."* — *"That was to confirm something. Let's go."* She does not explain.

### What the Players Are Being Shown

Mercedes's purpose this chapter is to *show* the players the public face — the legitimate university, real students, real lectures — so that when they see the vault tomorrow, they understand why the Keepers cannot easily move what is below it. The university is not a sham. There are 230 enrolled students. They go to class. The vault is *underneath the country's first secular university*, and the Keepers have to pretend it isn't.

**SAN Check**: none — but a quiet POW or INT-based **Insight** roll, if the player engages: *the architecture is doing work.* The eleven-by-nine grid, the orientation of San Ignacio, the precise placement of the Cabildo, the position of the Riachuelo and the Río de la Plata — they form a containment geometry at the city scale. Whoever laid out Buenos Aires in 1580 was not a layman.

## SCENE 3 — FIRST GLIMPSE OF THE VAULT

### Setting Description

> *Mercedes leads you not to the marked door (Archivo Privado), but to a room three floors above it — a rector's office, currently empty, used for storage of broken furniture. She closes the door. She lifts a corner of a Persian rug. Beneath it: a brass disc, set flush into the floor, perhaps fifteen centimeters across. It looks like a carpenter's plumb-marker. It is not.*
>
> *"Pongan la mano sobre esto,"* she says. *"Una a la vez. No la dejen mucho rato."* — *"Put a hand on it. One at a time. Don't leave it long."*

**What the disc does**: It is a *witness-stone* — Keeper-made, brass over a core of *piedra parada* from the old Tehuelche binding tradition. When a Marked person rests a hand on it, the stone communicates the state of the vault below: cold and steady (containment intact), warm (containment under stress), or hot (containment failing). It does not speak. It is a thermometer for the binding.

**What it reads tonight**: Warm. Not hot. Warm.

Mercedes watches each player's face as they touch it. *"Sí. Eso me dijeron también."* — *"Yes. That's what they told me too."* She replaces the rug.

**SAN Check**: 0/1 — the warm stone, the implication of what *hot* would mean, the realization that the building you are standing in is in active failure mode and the students two floors below are studying Latin grammar.

## SCENE 4 — THE KEEPER MEETING HOUSE

The party arrives at Don Eladio Saráchaga's residence in the Barrio del Alto at dusk. Mercedes does not knock. She has a key.

### Setting Description

> *The house is a *casa chorizo* — the long, narrow style, three patios deep — set back from Calle Defensa behind a low wall. The first patio is for visitors who don't know it's a Keeper house: a parlor, a portrait of San Martín in a gilt frame, a clavichord nobody plays. The second patio is for Keeper business: a rectangular room with whitewashed walls, a long pine table, fourteen chairs, a fireplace that has not been lit in summer, and on the far wall a single iron-bound chest with three locks of different makes. The third patio has a vegetable garden and a well. The well is older than the city.*

**Who is present at the meeting**:

- **Don Eladio Saráchaga** — host. The dissenter.
- **Doña Mercedes** — sponsor of the players' presence. The advocate for moving the book.
- **Padre Ramón Albarrán** — the priest. Custodian of the archive's consecration.
- **Captain Juan Pablo Galíndez** — retired colonial officer, the Keepers' practical hand.
- **Ana Bermúdez** — junior Keeper. Cipher specialist. Recording secretary tonight.
- **Two empty chairs**. Mercedes does not say whose.

The number of empty chairs *matters*. There are normally seven seated Keepers. Two are unaccounted for. Mercedes says only: *"Esos no van a venir."* — *"Those two won't be coming."* She does not name them. (One is Quirce, who has been excluded for cause; the other is **Don Bartolomé Lacasa**, who is now in El Patrón's pocket and stays away to avoid being challenged on it.)

### NPC: DON ELADIO SARÁCHAGA

**Role**: The senior dissenter. The Keepers' institutional memory. The person who will argue hardest *against* moving the book.

**Appearance**: 71 years old, white-haired, lean. He was an officer in the late Spanish administration and a quiet supporter of the *cabildo abierto* of 1810 — a man who has changed political loyalty exactly once, at the moment that mattered, and never since. He wears a *frac* in the older cut, no jewelry, a single iron ring on his right hand that bears no insignia. The ring is a Keeper-mark, but you would have to know to look.

**Voice**: Castilian Spanish with old Buenos Aires inflections. Slow, deliberate. He is the kind of speaker who makes other people stop fidgeting.

**Mannerisms**:
- He pours water for guests himself. He does not have servants in this room.
- He writes in a small leather notebook continuously throughout meetings, in his own shorthand. The notebook is read by no one else, ever.
- When he disagrees, he becomes more polite, not less. The deeper the courtesy, the more total the opposition.

**Position on the book**: *"Mientras esté abajo, está. El movimiento es lo que él quiere."* — *"While it's below, it is. The movement is what he wants."* He has held this position for forty years. He held it through three previous attempts to relocate the book to safer institutions in the Vatican, in Coimbra, and (in 1810) on a British frigate. Each time, he has been right.

**What changes his mind, if anything**: Not argument. Evidence. Specifically — the witness-stone going from warm to hot, and a Keeper death he cannot explain by ordinary means.

**Possible Questions & Answers — Saráchaga**

*"What is your case for keeping it?"*
> *"Tres siglos de éxito."* — *"Three centuries of success."* *(He does not elaborate further unless pushed. If pushed:)* *"Cada vez que lo movieron en otro siglo, alguien murió que no debía morir. Y la mitad de las veces, lo que se quería evitar, ocurrió igual."* — *"Every time it has been moved, someone died who shouldn't have. And half the time, what they were trying to avoid happened anyway."*

*"What if the containment is failing?"*
> *(He looks at Mercedes.)* *"Mercedes dice que está. Yo confío en su mano. Pero la piedra está tibia, no caliente. Hay tiempo. Esa es la pregunta — ¿cuánto?"* — *"Mercedes says it is. I trust her hand. But the stone is warm, not hot. There is time. The question is — how much?"*

*"What do you think of us being here?"*
> *(He looks at the players for the first time directly.)* *"Ustedes son gente útil. También son gente que ha roto cosas que ahora no podemos arreglar. Las dos cosas son ciertas. Yo no creo que un grupo de gauchos marcados deba decidir el destino de un libro que estuvo en una bóveda mientras se construían los reinos. Pero su decisión va a contar. Esa es la realidad."*  — *"You are useful people. You are also people who have broken things we now can't fix. Both are true. I don't believe a group of Marked gauchos should be deciding the fate of a book that sat in a vault while kingdoms were built. But your decision will count. That is the reality."*

### NPC: PADRE RAMÓN ALBARRÁN

**Role**: The priest. Theological cover and consecration of the vault's Catholic surface.

**Appearance**: 54, Franciscan, brown habit. Bald with a fringe of grey. Heavy hands. He was a missionary among the Toba in the Chaco for fifteen years and came back with malaria he never quite shook — a tremor in the left hand when he is tired, which is always.

**Voice**: Soft, patient, Latin-accented Spanish. He thinks before answering and sometimes thinks too long.

**Mannerisms**:
- He clasps his hands in his lap and does not gesture.
- He answers theological questions with quotations from St. Augustine and practical questions with proverbs from Tobá oral tradition. He treats both as equally authoritative and is not joking.

**Position on the book**: He does not have one. He believes God has not yet revealed the right answer. He will support whatever the Keepers decide *only after* they have argued it through. He is a vote of conscience, and tonight his conscience is not yet decided.

**What he will say tonight, if directly asked**: *"He estado rezando por esto durante quince años. Dios todavía no me contesta sobre el libro. Cuando me conteste, voy a saber. Esta noche todavía no sé."* — *"I have been praying about this for fifteen years. God has not yet answered me about the book. When He does, I will know. Tonight I do not yet know."*

**Foreshadowing**: He is, mechanically, the swing vote in C11. Whoever convinces him decides whether the book is moved.

### NPC: CAPTAIN JUAN PABLO GALÍNDEZ

**Role**: The practical hand. Logistics, security, men under arms.

**Appearance**: 49, a colonial cavalry officer who fought against Whitelocke's English in 1807 and stayed neutral during the wars of independence. Spare frame, grey at the temples, a saber-cut scar along his jaw that he did not bother to hide. Plain dark coat. He is the only person in the room visibly armed (a small cavalry pistol at his belt, holstered).

**Voice**: Curt, matter-of-fact, military Spanish without ornament.

**Mannerisms**:
- He stands rather than sits when he speaks at length.
- He counts on his fingers when discussing logistics — not from limited intellect, from habit.

**Position on the book**: He believes the book should be moved *only if a credible plan exists for it to arrive*. If the plan is half-baked, he votes against. If it is sound, he is the man who runs it. He is not sentimental.

**What he will say tonight**: *"Si lo mueven, lo mueven con quince hombres armados, dos rutas falsas, y un barco esperando. Cualquier cosa menos, no salgo. Yo no muero por un plan flojo."* — *"If you move it, you move it with fifteen armed men, two decoy routes, and a ship waiting. Less than that, I don't go. I'm not dying for a sloppy plan."*

**Foreshadowing**: His estimate of "fifteen armed men" is the floor for the Chapter 12 transport. Players who help recruit men in C11 contribute directly to the heist's success.

### NPC: ANA BERMÚDEZ

**Role**: Junior Keeper. Cipher and correspondence. Tonight's recording secretary.

**Appearance**: 26, the only person in the room under fifty. She wears a dark dress without ornament. Her hands are ink-stained. She writes left-handed, in a code Saráchaga taught her, in a leather book that nobody else can read.

**Voice**: Quiet, careful, Buenos Aires-Castilian with the precision of someone whose handwriting is her livelihood.

**Mannerisms**:
- She does not look up from her notebook when others speak.
- She does look up at moments of genuine emphasis — and her gaze, when it comes, is sharper than the room expects.

**The truth about Ana** (Keeper only): She has been corresponding with Aldao Quirce for the past six months. She believes she is gathering information about Quirce *for* the Keepers. In fact she has been *fed* information by Quirce, which she has dutifully written into her records. The records are now slightly compromised — some Keeper deliberations are tinted by Quirce's frame. She does not know this. She is not yet a traitor; she is a leak.

**What she will do this chapter**: She takes minutes. She watches the players carefully. She asks one quiet question, near the end of the meeting, addressed not to the room but to Mercedes: *"Si la piedra se pone caliente esta semana, ¿quién decide cuándo movemos?"* — *"If the stone becomes hot this week, who decides when we move?"* This is a question Quirce told her to ask.

A **Hard Psychology** roll on Ana during the meeting picks up that one of her notes is a direct response to a question that hasn't been asked yet. She is, very subtly, ahead of the conversation. She doesn't know the players have noticed.

### THE DEBATE — STRUCTURE OF THE MEETING

The meeting runs for two hours. Mercedes opens. Saráchaga responds. Galíndez asks the operational questions. Albarrán prays. Ana writes.

**Mercedes's argument**:
> *"La piedra está tibia. Eusebio Valdez vivió. Los gauchos han visto a El Patrón. Quirce nos vendió. La luz mala se ve dentro de las paredes de la ciudad. Tenemos que mover el libro antes de que él nos obligue a moverlo."* — *"The stone is warm. Eusebio Valdez lived. The gauchos have seen El Patrón. Quirce sold us out. La Luz Mala is being seen inside the city walls. We have to move the book before he forces us to."*

**Saráchaga's counter**:
> *"Forzar es exactamente lo que él quiere. La piedra estuvo tibia en 1810. La piedra estuvo tibia en 1776. Cada vez que se discutió moverlo, era porque alguien lo estaba pidiendo. La pregunta es: ¿quién está pidiendo esto, en realidad?"* — *"Forcing is exactly what he wants. The stone was warm in 1810. The stone was warm in 1776. Every time moving it has been discussed, it was because someone was asking. The question is: who is really asking this?"*

**The players are asked their position**. Mercedes brings them in: *"Ustedes han visto su mano de cerca. Cuéntenles."* — *"You have seen his hand up close. Tell them."* This is a real player moment. The Keepers will weigh what the players say. **Persuade / Credit Rating / Psychology rolls** matter here — not for a binary outcome, but for how many Keepers are receptive in C11.

**The vote tonight**: There is no vote tonight. Saráchaga will not allow a vote until *"la piedra esté caliente, no tibia,"* — until the stone is *hot*, not *warm*. He is buying time. Mercedes accepts this. The Keepers go home around midnight. The players are escorted back to Inocencia's boarding house by Captain Galíndez, who walks the streets like a man who reads them as a battlefield.

## SCENE 5 — THE STREETS AT NIGHT

While Galíndez walks the players back, four small things happen:

1. **A figure on the opposite corner.** Not following. *Watching.* They cross Defensa and the figure does not move. Galíndez does not turn his head, but his hand drops to his pistol. *"Sigan caminando, no miren."* — *"Keep walking, don't look."*

2. **A flicker in a third-floor window.** A pale yellowish-white light, a single pulse, in a window of a house the party passes. **Spot Hidden**: the window is open, no candle. La Luz Mala has just appeared inside a private home in the Barrio del Alto. It is not a coincidence.

3. **The bell of the Cabildo strikes midnight.** Galíndez stops counting steps and counts strikes. There are *eleven*. He looks up sharply. *"Ese reloj toca doce. Siempre toca doce."* — *"That clock strikes twelve. It always strikes twelve."* He does not know what the eleven means. Players who have read Doña Mercedes' margin notes from Arc 2 may: the city's containment grid is eleven by nine. Something has reduced it to eleven by eight, briefly. Then twelve strikes — the missing one comes late, with the next breath. The containment recovers.

4. **A tribal ally is gone.** When the players reach Inocencia's boarding house, one of the surviving tribal allies they brought into the city — name them based on Arc 3 outcomes — is *not* there. Their bedroll is rolled neatly. Their gear is gone. Inocencia, frightened: *"Salieron temprano, dijeron que iban al sur."* — *"They left early, they said they were going south."* South means the Riachuelo. South means the Cursed Gaucho's tannery. The ally has gone to him. Or *been* gone to him. The distinction will matter in C11.

**SAN Check**: 0/1 — for the cumulative wrongness of the city at midnight.

## SCENE 6 — DAWN: A KEEPER IS DEAD

This is the chapter's closing scene. It is non-negotiable.

The players are woken at first light. Galíndez is at the front door with a man on each shoulder. He says one sentence: *"Saráchaga está muerto. Vengan."* — *"Saráchaga is dead. Come."*

### Setting Description

> *The casa chorizo at dawn looks different from the night before. The first patio is full of *vigilantes urbanos* — the city's new municipal police, founded last year, still unsure of its own authority — and a pair of priests from San Ignacio who are pretending not to be Keepers. The second patio is closed off. Galíndez walks you straight through.*
>
> *The long pine table is as it was last night. Saráchaga's notebook is on the table, closed, in the same position he left it. His chair is overturned. His body is on the floor between his chair and the iron-bound chest. He is on his back. There is no blood, no bruise, no visible wound. His eyes are open. His right hand is clenched around the iron ring. The ring is on the floor — he tore it off his finger before he died.*

**What killed him**:

- A **Medicine** roll: heart failure, but not natural — the timing is wrong, the body is too composed, no autonomic struggle. Saráchaga went *between one breath and the next*, with full awareness.
- A **Spot Hidden** roll on the body: the soles of his shoes have a dust that does not belong indoors. He was outside, briefly, in the third patio (the well, the older one). He was at the well at some point in the night.
- A **Hard Spot Hidden** on the well: a brass disc, identical to the witness-stone in the rector's office, at the bottom of the well, three meters down, in cold water. The disc is *hot*. It has been hot for hours. Saráchaga went out to check it because Mercedes' stone in the rector's office was warm, not hot — but his private stone, this one, was telling a different story. The vault's containment is *worse* than Mercedes thinks.
- A **Hard Occult or Cthulhu Mythos** roll on the absence of wound: this looks, mechanically, exactly like what happens when an entity makes contact with a Marked person and the body can't bear the witness. Saráchaga was, secretly, also Marked — from a closing he was present for in 1774, when he was 24 years old. He has been a Keeper since.
- The clenched ring: he tore it off because it was burning him. The Keeper-mark, in proximity to El Patrón's reach, became hot.

**What the body says, that he meant the players to find**: His notebook, on the table. The last entry, in his shorthand. **Ana Bermúdez** is in the second patio when the players arrive. She is white-faced. She is not yet under suspicion (she had a clear alibi — at home, asleep, witnessed). She offers to translate the shorthand. *Players who do not yet trust her can refuse and try to find someone else to translate, but the only other shorthand reader in the city is Quirce.*

If Ana translates: the entry reads *"Caliente, no tibia. La piedra del pozo está caliente. Hay menos tiempo del que pensé. Mercedes tenía razón. Movámoslo."* — *"Hot, not warm. The well-stone is hot. There is less time than I thought. Mercedes was right. Let's move it."*

He died converted. He died agreeing with Mercedes. He died alone.

**SAN Check**: 1/1D4 — Saráchaga's body, the silent geometry of his last hour, the realization that the man who was the strongest argument against moving the book was given the fact that he was wrong, and then killed before he could speak.

### Observable consequences (D5) — the city as witness

Use these in passing to keep Buenos Aires a *living* containment grid. None require rolls; describe in transit.

- **Floor tiles in the Manzana de las Luces**: When a Marked PC walks the long corridor from the rector's office to the second patio, their footfalls echo with a *second click* — a half-beat after each step, very faint. The tiles are laid on the 1610 binding pattern. Their footsteps are being doubled by the geometry. The unmarked PCs hear normal echoes. (The Marked PC will recognize the rhythm — it is the same twelve-syllable pattern from the Salamanca tunnels in C5.)
- **Lamp oil in the city**: At any inn, café, or boarding house the players sit in, the oil lamps burn with a *taller flame* on the side facing the Manzana de las Luces. Inocencia notes this matter-of-factly: *"Las lámparas miran a la Manzana. Siempre fue así."* — *"The lamps look at the Manzana. It has always been so."*
- **Pigeons at the Plaza de Mayo**: The pigeons do not land within a four-meter circle around the Cabildo's clock tower. *Ever.* The vendors who sell maize will tell players this with a shrug if asked.
- **Saráchaga's well, after his death**: The water is now *clear* — no longer cold and silty as a porteño well should be. A Marked PC looking down into it sees the brass disc at the bottom and, around the disc, faint concentric rings in the water that do not move. The water is reading the disc the way the Salamanca canteens read the stone (D5, C5).

## END OF CHAPTER 10

The chapter ends in the second patio, with the body on the floor, the *vigilantes* outside, Mercedes arriving and going still in the doorway, and the players holding the dead man's notebook.

**Mercedes, after a long silence**:
> *"Ya está. Mañana votamos."* — *"It's done. Tomorrow we vote."*

The decision the Keepers were going to delay for weeks — until the stone was hot — has been made for them. The stone *is* hot. They just didn't know it. Saráchaga did. He was killed for knowing it.

## GM NOTES

**The chapter is two halves**: the slow first half (city-as-character, the Keepers as people) and the fast last hour (Saráchaga's death). Don't hurry the first half. The Keepers must feel like *people you know* before one of them dies, or the death is just plot.

**Saráchaga's body is the campaign's first city-scale supernatural event**: He died with his eyes open, no wound, holding a ring that was burning him. This is the kind of death the players will not be able to investigate by ordinary means. Frustration is the point. The city does not work the way the pampa worked. You cannot follow tracks here.

**Ana Bermúdez should not be exposed yet**: Her shorthand translation in the second patio is correct, mostly. She gives the players the real last entry. She has not yet decided to betray; she has been *used*. Save her exposure for C11. Tonight she is just shaken and helpful.

**The witness-stones are the campaign's new mechanic**: One in the rector's office (warm). One in Saráchaga's well (hot). At least one more that the Keepers don't know about (Quirce has placed his own). Players who think to ask "are there others" are rewarded with a search — running into Albarrán, Galíndez, or eventually the vault itself in C11.

**The eleven-strike clocks**: The Cabildo at midnight, the Cabildo at dawn — both miscount the hour by one, very briefly, before correcting. The city's containment grid is *fluttering*. By C12, several clocks in the city will mistime the hour at once, and the players will know the geometry is breaking.

**El Patrón has not appeared on stage**: He has been a carriage, a window, a death. Keep him off stage in this chapter. C11 is the chapter where he becomes a presence; C12 is where he becomes a person.

**Tribal allies in the city**: Treat the missing ally as a real concern. The Cursed Gaucho has been recruiting them off the books — he is preparing his last stand and wants people who know the geometry helping him. Players will encounter this in C11.

**The Marks**: Don't activate them dramatically this chapter. Use them as awareness — the Marked players notice the city geometry, sense the warm stone before non-Marked, feel Saráchaga's death-room before they enter it. The Marks are how the players see what other people miss.
