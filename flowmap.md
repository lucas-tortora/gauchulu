# LA TIERRA MALA — CAMPAIGN FLOWMAP

A single-document GM reference for the full 12-chapter campaign. Each chapter is a *narrative unit*, not a play session — a chapter can take one play night, two, or be split across breaks. Use this map to navigate the campaign's threads, escalation curve, and decision points without rereading every chapter file.

> Detailed prep lives in the per-arc folders: `01-la-tierra-mala/`, `02-la-salamanca/`, `03-the-great-sleeper/`, `04-buenos-aires-and-the-heist/`. This document is the index and through-line.

> Visual handouts live in `assets/`. Six SVGs, each printable at the table:

## CAMPAIGN AT A GLANCE

- **Setting**: Pampa argentina, 1821. Estancia (frontier *campaña*) → folklore underground → Andean cult → Buenos Aires (Rivadavia-era port, newly secularized Manzana de las Luces).
- **Premise**: Three gauchos hired as hunters discover the corruption is the patrón's own doing, then chase a wider conspiracy to its end at the Necronomicon.
- **Length**: 12 chapters across 4 arcs. Estimated 12–20 play sessions depending on table pace.
- **Final question**: Should the Necronomicon remain hidden, be used, or be destroyed? Who pays the price?

## MECHANICAL SHORTHAND

- **Mark of Tindalos (mechanical)**: 2d6 in tense moments. Double 1 = Hound for 1d3 rounds.
- **Mark of Tindalos (perceptive — the leverage rule)**: From C3 onward, Marked PCs *see geometry*. They do not need NPCs to translate diagrams, alignments, or ritual shapes — they perceive them as felt wrongness or felt completeness. Mythos rolls about pattern in real space are at -1 difficulty step. They cannot read text; they read shape. **This is the campaign's interpretive engine, replacing the solver-NPC pattern.** Active "look hard" use raises the 2d6 trigger threshold to double 1 or double 2 for that scene. See `overview.md` *Mark of Tindalos* and `assets/geometric-perception-cues.md`.
- **Witness-stone**: brass disc; cool / warm / hot. Tells the vault's containment state from a distance. Introduced C10.
- **Geometry contest** (during ritual phases): each phase = 1 contest. Difficulty modified by outer ring integrity, the focus's condition, asymmetries. See C8–C9.
- **Sanity bands**:
  - Discovery (C1–C2): 0/1 hits only.
  - Catastrophe (C3): 0/1 to 1/1D6.
  - Investigation / Eve (C4–C7): 0/1D4 to 0/1D6 max.
  - Ritual (C8–C9): 0/1D4 per phase, 1/1D6 at climaxes.
  - City (C10–C12): 0/1 sustained, 1/1D6 at El Patrón scenes, 1/1D8 at the dock.
- **The Choice (C12)**: three options, each with named cost.
  - Hidden — Albarrán dies; Mercedes loses an eye. El Patrón withdraws.
  - Used — one PC reads the book and is permanently changed. El Patrón is bound. Book remains.
  - Destroyed — book ends; El Patrón ends; Marked PCs pay (death / permanent loss / scarred-but-alive). Tradition lost.

## RUNNING NOTES — KEEP ON HAND

- **Don't explain**. Let outcomes prove themselves. The campaign's primary horror is *acting on incomplete knowledge*. If players figure something out, reward; if they don't, the next chapter teaches them by cost.
- **The polite villain is the worst villain**. El Patrón is courteous in C11's parlor, on Calle Belgrano in C12, on the dock. Players should *almost agree* with him. That's the design.
- **Allies pay**. Albarrán, Saráchaga, Saúl, the Cursed Gaucho, possibly Mercedes, possibly the Wrong Returned. Make each death named and felt; do not montage.
- **The Marks pay**. They are introduced in C3 and consumed in C12. Carry them through every chapter as ambient awareness — a character feature, not just a mechanic.
- **The geometry is the through-line**. Every horror in the campaign is the same shape. Drop a single line about angles in every chapter; never lecture about it.

## ATMOSPHERIC RUNNERS — THREE QUIET MOTIFS

These are the campaign's three recurring atmospheric threads. Each has at least one specific landing per chapter. None are mechanically rewarded; they are texture. Players who *notice* should be quietly rewarded with a small Sanity buffer (a single point banked against the next loss). Players who don't notice are still living in the world the runners describe.

### The Goat in the San Ruiz Well

Introduced in **C2**. A nanny goat lives on a fallen-masonry ledge inside the dry well at the centre of San Ruiz village. She does not eat. She does not bleed if cut. She is unhurt. She watches. She is not malevolent.

| Chapter | Where she appears |
|---|---|
| C2 | First seen. Players try to rescue her; she walks back to the well herself. Rosa's only line about her: *"Está ahí desde antes de que se fueran. Yo le tiro pasto a veces. No le hace falta."* |
| C8 | The party rides through San Ruiz before Coypac. She is still there. No one mentions her. |
| C9 | The ride east passes through San Ruiz. A Marked PC, looking down at her, sees for the first time that **she has six pupils — three in each eye, in a line.** No one else sees this. |
| C12 (epilogue) | *"The new families know about her and leave her there."* |

She is the campaign's most patient witness. **No NPC ever brings her up directly.** Players who ask Rosa what she is get only the line above. Players who ask Mercedes get a pause and *"Algunas cosas no son para nosotros."*

### The Dust from the Pit

Introduced in **C2** (Rosa names it). Fine grey particulate from the original carving's limestone, ground over centuries by the geometry. Settles on clothing and does not wash out. Mercedes (in C5 letter) confirms what it is. Other Marked-adjacent NPCs recognize it without naming it.

| Chapter | Who notices |
|---|---|
| C2 | Rosa: *"Estuvieron en el pozo. Lo huelo. Cargan el polvo encima."* |
| C3 | Kuyen looks at a sleeve cuff. *"Walked through the pit too. I see it on you."* |
| C4 | Silvio: *"Tienen el polvo. Como yo."* — the first naming as kinship. |
| C7 | Mercedes shakes hands with each player; she pauses on the third hand and rubs her thumb across the cuff. She does not name it. |
| C10 | Inocencia, hanging the players' jackets to dry, beats the dust out with a stick at her doorstep. The dust on her doorstep does not blow away. It sits. She sweeps it into a tin and saves it. (She does not know why.) |
| C11 | Mercedes notices her own forearm has dust on it, picked up from a player's coat-cuff at the meeting last night. She brushes it off carefully, into a small folded paper. She files the paper in the archive. She does this without comment. |
| C12 | At the moment of the binding/destroy/use, the dust on the players' clothing **lifts off** — visibly — and joins the geometry forming around the dock. It is *taken back* by the shape. Investigators who have been carrying it the longest feel briefly lighter. |

### The Low Vibration

Introduced in **C1**. A sub-audible vibration that is felt more than heard, at any active geometry. **Players who describe their investigator noticing it should be rewarded with one banked Sanity point.** Awareness is protective.

| Chapter | Where the vibration is present |
|---|---|
| C1 | At the burial pit. Strongest within the body-arrangement perimeter. It silences when the geometry is being scraped apart. |
| C2 | In the chapel, during La Llorona's manifestation. It is the chapel itself remembering. |
| C3 | At the creek bed during the counter-ritual. The four cardinal fires sit at the corners of a rectangle of vibration. Players feel it in their sternums. |
| C4 | In Don Eusebio's locked study. The manuscript itself carries it; opening the manuscript intensifies it. |
| C5 | At the Saens stone, descending into the bone chamber. The ladder rungs hum faintly. |
| C6 | In Don Eusebio's study during Quirce's mediumship moment. The vibration *stops* for the ten seconds Quirce is speaking with someone else's voice. |
| C7 | At the camp the night before the closing. Continuous, uneven. |
| C8 | During the closing — escalating with each phase, peaking at Phase 5, falling silent at the moment the geometry locks. |
| C9 | Silent for the first time in the campaign. **The players notice the absence**, not the presence. The pampa is the quietest it has been since C1. |
| C10 | Picks up again, faintly, in Buenos Aires. Strongest in the cloister of San Ignacio. |
| C11 | In the vault. The limestone walls carry it the way the Saens stone did. Marked PCs feel it in their teeth. |
| C12 | Maximum throughout the chapter. Activates in the city grid as the carriage moves. Silenced at the moment of the chosen ending — Hide reduces it to baseline, Use shifts it to the Reader's body specifically, Destroyed *ends* it for everyone permanently. |

The vibration is the entity's baseline. It is the campaign's continuous-presence equivalent of a weather sound. Drop one line about it per chapter. **In C9 (the silence) and C12 (the resolution)**, it should be a full beat the GM lingers on.

## THE THREE NAMED PRAYERS

The campaign has three prayers that should be **named aloud by the GM** when they are said in the fiction. Players will remember named things. These are atmospheric props, not mechanics.

### *Por la tierra que fue buena* — the gaucho's prayer

A short Spanish lay-prayer that rural workers in the south plains say over land that has gone wrong — graves, droughts, places where animals die unexpectedly. It is folk Catholicism with a pre-Christian root. The full text is six lines; in play it is usually heard as the first two:

> *"Por la tierra que fue buena, y por la tierra que se cansó. Que la tierra descanse, y que nosotros pasemos ligeros."* — *"For the land that was good, and for the land that grew tired. May the land rest, and may we pass lightly."*

| Chapter | Who says it |
|---|---|
| C1 | Tomás, before he and the investigators enter the burial pit. He removes his hat and speaks the first two lines. |
| C8 | Don Eusebio, in the outer ring, before the closing begins. He has not said the prayer in twenty years. He stumbles on the third line. |
| C12 (Hide ending only) | Albarrán, on the dock, before he speaks the *Hic manet*. The Spanish prayer first, the Latin second. |

### The Mapudungun counter-chant — *Antu kishu inchiñ ñi pewenmu*

The chant Kuyen and her people use at the closing ritual. It is in Mapudungun. The full chant is approximately twenty lines; in play it is heard as a single repeated line:

> *"Antu kishu inchiñ ñi pewenmu. Antu kishu, lan amukey."*  — *"The sun [is] alone in our araucaria-grove. The sun alone, the dying does not pass."*

| Chapter | Where heard |
|---|---|
| C3 | Kuyen and the six tribe members chant continuously during the binding ritual. Marked PCs hear it in their dreams afterward, in the same cadence. |
| C8 | Kuyen leads the chant during the closing. At the moment of Phase 5, *every NPC in the outer ring* — including Don Eusebio, including Mercedes, including Quirce if he is still there — joins the line *Antu kishu, lan amukey*. None of them know they have learned it. They learned it from the geometry. |
| C12 (Destroyed ending only) | Kuyen, if alive, leads the chant during Mercedes's reading of the destroy-ritual. The chant is the line the destroy-page is folded around. |

### Rosa's silent prayer

Rosa speaks no prayer aloud in the campaign. She *does*, however, kneel in the burned chapel of San Ruiz at the end of C2, after the players have left, and pray silently for sixty seconds with her hands on Dolores's grave-mark. **The prayer is named in the GM notes only.** Rosa's prayer is *Por las que vinieron antes y por las que vendrán después* — *"For those who came before and for those who come after."* It is her own composition. She has been saying it daily for fourteen months. **It is what is sustaining the partial binding** in the chapel walls, alongside the scratched symbols. Without Rosa, the chapel would have failed entirely a year ago.

| Chapter | Where it operates (silent, not heard) |
|---|---|
| C2 | Rosa kneels after the players leave the chapel. She says it once. The walls hold for another month. |
| C9 | Rosa says it as the players ride away east. She does not know what they are doing, but she says it for them. |
| C12 (epilogue) | If the players ever return to San Ruiz — alive — Rosa is still there, still saying it daily, still sustaining the chapel. She does not name the prayer to them. **A Marked PC who lingers at the chapel doorway in the epilogue feels the prayer as a felt geometry, not as words.** They will know what is keeping the place quiet.

The prayer is an example of *folk practice as functional ritual*. Rosa is not magical. She is consistent. The geometry recognizes consistency.
