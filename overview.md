# LA TIERRA MALA

## Core Premise

Three gauchos begin as hired hunters tasked with pursuing local tribes blamed for cattle deaths and abandoned settlements. Their investigation reveals that El Patrón is behind the corruption of the land, the spreading rituals, and the manipulation of every major faction.

The true conflict centers around the Necronomicon, secretly contained beneath the recently founded University of Buenos Aires. El Patrón wants the Keepers of the book to move it—because only during transport can it be stolen.

The Cult of the Great Sleeper seeks the same book to awaken Azathoth.

The players begin by chasing the wrong enemy and end by deciding who should possess the worst book in the world.

# RECURRING SUPERNATURAL ELEMENTS

## Mark of Tindalos

Any player wounded by the Hound may be marked.

**Rule (mechanical)**:
Roll 2d6 during high-tension moments.
Double 1 = Hound manifests for 1d3 rounds.

**Rule (perceptive — the leverage rule)**:

The Mark is *not* just a sword hanging over the player. It is the campaign's primary in-fiction interpretive tool. From the moment a player takes a Mark in C3 onward, that player **perceives geometry the way other people perceive grammar**: angles, alignments, ritual shapes, and the seams between this world and the entity's are *visible* to them, not as text but as a felt wrongness, a sensed completeness, a recognition of pattern. In play this means:

- A Marked player walks into a room and *knows* whether the floor plan is on a binding-square or off it. Not from a dice roll. They look, they know.
- A Marked player looking at a ritual object does not need an NPC to translate it. The geometry "reads" to them, partially, the way a musician hears chord changes without translating to letters.
- Marked players can attempt **Cthulhu Mythos rolls at one difficulty step lower** for any check that involves seeing pattern, geometry, or alignment in real space. They cannot read text or untranslated language with this — only shape.
- When the Hound is near, the geometry distorts visibly to them *before* the dice are rolled. This is the in-fiction warning that pairs with the 2d6 trigger.

**Why this matters at the table**: Without this rule, players have to be *told* what every diagram and ritual means by an NPC (Rosa, Kuyen, Mercedes), which makes them passive consumers of revelation. With this rule, the Marked players are the campaign's interpretive engine — they *see* things the GM doesn't have to over-explain. Unmarked players do not have this; they ask questions of the Marked players. The Mark becomes the campaign's internal teacher, replacing the solver-NPC pattern.

**What the Mark is *not***:
- It is not telepathy. They do not hear the entity's thoughts.
- It is not omniscience. They see *geometry*, not *intent*. They can see a binding is incomplete; they cannot see *who* placed it or *why*.
- It is not a substitute for investigation. Many of the campaign's clues are textual, social, or historical. The Mark only handles *space*.

**Cost**: The same perception that lets them read shapes also makes them increasingly vulnerable. Each scene in which they actively use geometric perception (player chooses to "look hard") raises the 2d6 trigger threshold for that scene from "double 1" to "double 1 or double 2." The Mark is the campaign's most powerful tool *and* its largest risk vector. Players who lean on it pay for it.

This rule is referenced by chapter as **the Mark = perception**. See `assets/geometric-perception-cues.md` for the trigger sheet.
