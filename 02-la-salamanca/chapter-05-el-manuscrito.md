# CHAPTER 5 — EL MANUSCRITO Y LOS QUE VOLVIERON
### *The Manuscript & The Returned*

**Chapter Goal**: Understand the full history of the scar. Meet the Wrong Returned. Locate the original wound in the land — and learn what it asks of those who would close it.
**Emotional Arc**: Comprehension → Weight of History → The horror of being chosen
**Key Seed**: The geometry is older than memory. The people who came back wrong are not lost — they are held.

## OPENING SITUATION

The investigators have the manuscript and the knowledge that Don Eusebio must stand in the closing ritual's outer ring. They have also seen the Wrong Returned at the fence line.

This chapter moves outward from the estancia — following Saens's route in reverse, toward the Jesuit mission ruins where the original carving was found. The Wrong Returned will follow. Silvio will watch from the fence until he can't hold back any longer, then walk south — not toward the ruins, toward the pit. Keep track of where Silvio goes.

**Parallel thread**: Kuyen and Nahuel are not idle. If investigators check in with the camp (a half-day's ride from the estancia now), Kuyen is preparing the ritual components and watching Nahuel. She confirms: the two approaches they've completed have the entity contained but pressed. *"It's pushing. Every night harder than the last. We need the pit."*

## SCENE 1 — THE WRONG RETURNED

### First Approach

Héctor and Marta spent the night at the fence line — standing, as near as anyone can tell, without sleeping. In the morning they are still there. When investigators approach them in daylight:

> *They turn to face you before you call out. Simultaneously. Not startled — they knew you were coming. Up close, they look exactly as Rosa described: they eat, they answer questions, they stand in sunlight without apparent discomfort. But there is something in the focus of their attention that is wrong in a way that takes a moment to name. They are looking at you, and also looking at something that occupies the same space as you but is not quite you — a geometry of your position in the space, rather than your face.*

**Appearance**:
- **Héctor Ríos**, 54. A former farmer from San Ruiz, wide-shouldered, graying. He was considered steady — not smart but reliable. Now he responds to questions with precise answers and no small talk. He has lost the social texture around his speech: the *"pos"*, the pauses, the indirect approaches. He just answers.
- **Marta Ríos** (his wife, no relation to Héctor — the surname coincidence was a village joke), 43. She was the village teacher and midwife. Calm by profession, she is calmer now in a way that is not professional. She is the more present of the two — occasionally she slips into something that was Marta, a moment of expression that crosses her face and is gone.

**What They Know**:
They don't know everything. They know what the entity showed them — which is geometrically accurate but not experientially interpretable. They speak about it in terms of architecture and space, not sensation.

*"It isn't underground,"* Héctor says. *"That was wrong. It's in the angles. It lives in the places where one surface meets another. The pit is just where the shape is biggest — where there's enough angle for it to reach through."*

*"There are others like us,"* Marta says, meaning Silvio, whom they have sensed but not met. *"Not many. The ones it touched but didn't take. We're still here. But we're also partly there."*

*"We came back because it's almost time,"* Héctor says. *"We felt it from Tres Lagunas. The closing, or the opening. One of them is going to happen. We need to be at the geometry when it does."*

**What They Want**: To be at the pit for the final event — closing or opening. They don't have a preference. They are not aligned with the entity; they are simply anchored to the geometry. Their presence at the closing ritual will matter (see Chapter 9).

**The question no one wants to ask**: What will happen to them when the geometry is sealed? Marta answers this if directly asked, without apparent distress: *"I don't know. If the shape closes, maybe we close with it. Maybe we come back. We decided it doesn't matter. We need to be there."*

**SAN Check**: 0/1D3 (sustained conversation with someone who is and isn't a person)

**Possible Questions & Answers — The Wrong Returned**

*"Do you remember what happened to you?"*
> Héctor: *"I remember walking south. The night was very clear. And then it was like — like someone showed me a map of a place I'd been trying to describe without words. It was a relief, for a moment. And then I was back and it was morning."*
> Marta: *"I remember it differently. There was no relief in mine. Only size. Something very large noticing something very small."*

*"Are you in pain?"*
> Héctor: *"No."* *(Said too quickly and too finally.)*
> Marta: *(A long pause.)* *"I don't feel pain the way I used to. I'm not sure if that's better."*

*"Can you be helped?"*
> Marta: *"You could close the geometry. Maybe that ends this. Or maybe this is what we are now and closing it just removes the sound."* *(She touches her own face as if checking it's still there.)* *"Close it anyway."*

## SCENE 2 — THE ROAD TO THE RUINS

The Jesuit mission ruins are a day's ride east and slightly south — Padre Saens's notes give landmarks (a forked river, a mesa that looks like a tooth) that are still accurate. The Wrong Returned walk alongside the party. They don't require water stops. They keep pace without apparent effort.

### Landscape Changes

> *The land between the estancia and the ruins is ordinary pampa — flat, dry grass, occasional low scrub. But if you travel with Héctor and Marta, you begin to see it through their attention. They note corners. Not dramatically — a glance when the road bends, a moment of stillness where a mesa casts a shadow in a right angle. They are reading the geometry of the land the way a cattleman reads grass for moisture. The land is full of angles they have learned to see.*

**Optional encounter — the road**:
During the night camp on the road, a Luz Mala appears to the south — not orbiting, just stationary, about 200 meters distant. Watching. It has followed them from the estancia. Héctor watches it without apparent fear. He says: *"It's a sensor. Not a threat unless you disturb the geometry."* He has known this since his night in the pasture. He learned it the same way he learned everything since then — from the inside.

The Luz Mala tracks the party all the way to the ruins.

## SCENE 3 — THE JESUIT MISSION RUINS

### Setting Description

> *The mission was abandoned in 1767 when the Jesuits were expelled from the Spanish colonies. What remains: the foundation stones of four buildings, a broken bell tower still partially standing, walls that reach chest-height and no further. The stone is pale limestone, different from the local rock — it was carried here. Whoever built this chose this location deliberately.*
>
> *The vegetation inside the ruins is different from outside. Denser, greener despite the season. Things grow here that shouldn't grow this far south — a fig tree, improbably, in the corner of what was the chapel. As if the land here is more fértil.*
>
> *That word. That word again.*

**Keeper Notes**:
- The bell tower still standing is the most intact structure. The bell itself fell and lies in the grass, half-buried, inscribed with the date 1749 — twelve years before the mission was built. The bell was brought from somewhere else.
- The chapel foundation has carved stones integrated into the floor — worked into the construction deliberately, flush with the other stones but different: the limestone has the geometry carved into it. These are the original carvings Saens found and documented. The mission was built *on* them, incorporating them into the Christian site — as Saens describes in the manuscript.

### The Carving

> *When you find the carved stone — and you will find it, because Héctor walks directly to it — it is both smaller and more precise than you expected. Perhaps sixty centimeters across, recessed into the floor of what was the chapel. The carving is deep and clean: the same angular-circular geometry, but here it is complete. Both the opening diagram and the counter-diagram, rendered simultaneously — one inside the other, interacting. Like a lock and key carved into the same stone.*
>
> *It is, without question, the most precise thing you have ever seen made by hand.*

> **Visual handout**: see `assets/dual-diagram.svg`. Print it large for the table — A4 minimum, A3 if possible. This is the carved stone. This is what Marta finger-traces in the air at midday. This is what the players will see at chamber-floor scale, in bones, in the bone chamber. This is what the city of Buenos Aires will *become* in C12. Show it, do not describe it.

![The dual diagram — opening and closing as one geometry](../assets/dual-diagram.svg)

**Skill checks**:
| Skill | Result |
|---|---|
| Occult | The dual diagram is not two competing symbols. It is one symbol — the opening and closing are two aspects of the same geometry, the way a door and a doorframe are two aspects of the same architectural intent. The opening is not wrong. It is incomplete. |
| Cthulhu Mythos | The geometry predates human habitation of this region by a very long time. The question of who carved it — before Saens, before the Tehuelche, before anyone — has no answer in any tradition this character knows. |
| History | The limestone this stone is made from does not exist within 200 kilometers. It was brought here, from the north. The carving is not in the stone — the stone was carved elsewhere and installed here. It was transported to this specific location. |
| Art/Craft (Drawing) | The precision of the carving is geometric in the mathematical sense — the angles are exact. Not approximately exact. Exact. The tools available at any period of human history should not produce this quality of line. |

**Héctor's observation**: He kneels beside the stone and places his hands on it — he is the only one who touches it without hesitation. He says: *"This is where the shape lives. The pit is just where someone tore a hole in it."*

**Marta's observation**: She stands at the door of the ruined chapel and looks at the stone from across the room. *"Padre Saens was right about one thing. It isn't the devil. But he was wrong about another. He said it was a wound. It isn't. It's a door. The wound is what we did to it."*

## DEEP READ: THE MANUSCRIPT — CHAPTER 5 ADDITIONS

If investigators spend time in the ruins cross-referencing the manuscript with the carved stone, additional passages become legible or interpretable that weren't clear before:

**Page 72 (partially water-damaged — now partially reconstructable)**:
> *"Antu-Rayo told me the ones who touch the shape and survive become anchors. They hold the geometry in place against distortion. Without them, the closing cannot be maintained against the entity's resistance. I did not understand this at first. I understand it now. The people who went to the field at night and returned — they are not lost. They are necessary."*

This is the key revelation of Chapter 5: the Wrong Returned are not simply victims. They are **structural components of the closing ritual**. Their presence at the outer ring will help stabilize the geometry against the entity's pressure in a way that trained humans cannot replicate. They were made for this, in a sense — not intentionally, not justly, but functionally.

**Page 79 (final legible line)**:
> *"I could not close it alone. I put this where the next one will find it. God forgive the next one for what they'll need to do."*

**SAN Check** (reading this final line): 0/1 — the recognition of what Saens felt writing it, and what it means that investigators are the "next one."

> **Reader-path seed (Use ending, C12)**: The PC who reads page 79 *aloud at the table*, in the GM's hearing, has — without anyone explaining this to them, including the GM at the table — taken the first step into the **Reader role** that becomes available in C12 (Use ending). The PC does not know this. The Marked PCs do not see it. **Mercedes will recognize it later** — see C8 expansion, *Mercedes, Between Phases*. From this point forward, when the PC who read page 79 looks at *any* ritual text in the campaign (Saens's manuscript, Quirce's notebook, Mercedes's parchment, the binding section of the Necronomicón itself in C12), one specific thing happens that does not happen for the others: **the words stay in their head verbatim for the rest of the chapter, in Saens's voice**. They are remembering language they did not learn. This is the campaign's quietest seed and pays off in C12 when Mercedes hands the binding section of the Necronomicón to a PC and that PC realizes they already half-know what the page says.

> **Optional disambiguation**: If multiple PCs read page 79 aloud, the seed lands on the one who reads it *slowest*, with the most weight, in the most silence. Reader as a role is about willingness to bear, not skill — the seed identifies the PC who will be willing to pay in C12. If no PC reads it aloud (everyone reads silently, or skips this scene), the Reader path is *closed* in C12: only Hide and Destroyed are available. Mercedes will not offer Use to a table that did not earn it here. **This is the only campaign branch that can be closed by C5 player choices alone. Make a Keeper note about whether the seed was planted.**

## GM NOTES

**The Wrong Returned are the emotional center of this chapter**: They are the most human element in an increasingly inhuman situation — people who walked into something they didn't understand, survived it differently, and are now choosing to face what comes next. Their composure is not the entity's doing; it's their own. Treat them with the dignity their situation deserves.

**The ruins should feel ancient and heavy**: This is not a spooky ruin — there are no ghosts here, no traps. The horror is geological. Something was here before the Tehuelche, before the Jesuits, before anyone. The carefully transported limestone. The perfect precision of the carving. The impossible fig tree. The land that is inexplicably more fertile within the walls. Something made this place a nexus and then left the geometry here like a road sign.

**The manuscript payoff**: Investigators have carried this document since Chapter 4. Chapter 5 is where it pays off. Give them time with it. The Saens passages should land as genuine revelation — not just exposition, but the experience of someone who failed where the investigators might not. The parallel is earned.

**Héctor and Marta as structural elements**: Make sure investigators understand, by the end of this chapter, that the Wrong Returned must be present at the closing. Not as sacrifices — as anchors. This changes their status from victims-to-be-protected to agents-with-a-role. It's more complicated than either the horror of sacrificial logic or the simplicity of rescue.

**Quirce is coming**: Don Eusebio mentioned Quirce at the end of Chapter 4 with hesitation. Investigators who wrote to him, or checked in with the estancia, or had Tomás watching for arrivals should now learn: Quirce has been spotted on the road. He is one day away. This is the hook that drives them back toward the estancia and into Chapter 6.

## EXPANSION NOTES — BRIDGING TO THE OVERVIEW FRAMING

The detailed prep above runs the *El Manuscrito y los que volvieron* chapter: the Wrong Returned, the road east, the Jesuit ruins, the carved dual diagram. The `overview.md` framing of Arc 2 places this chapter as the **Salamanca underground** — bone chamber, whispering tunnels, recurring folklore horrors, first deeper Necronomicon reference. The Jesuit ruins map cleanly onto the surface entrance to La Salamanca. Use the scenes below to extend the existing prep with overview content.

### Reskinning summary

- **The Jesuit mission ruins** are the *outer* ritual grounds (overview). The **chapel floor and its carved limestone stone** is the entrance marker to La Salamanca proper. The descent into Salamanca occurs through this stone — but only with Héctor and Marta's help.
- **Héctor and Marta** (the Wrong Returned) are reframed in the overview as figures who have already been *partly* through the door. They are the campaign's first humans who have crossed the threshold and returned. Their geometric tracing (Marta's dawn ritual at the chapel doorway) is them reading the door's mechanism.
- **El Pombero** (introduced in C4 expansion) returns here as guide to the descent.
- **Doña Mercedes** (introduced in C4 expansion via her letter) replies. She arrives by post, not in person, but her voice is now a presence at the table.

### NEW SCENE — DESCENT AT THE CARVED STONE

Insert this after the manuscript deep-read and the night at the ruins, before the Kuyen interlude. The players have spent twelve hours at the ruins; they understand the dual diagram. Now Héctor or Marta can show them what the diagram does.

> *Marta walks to the chapel floor at midday — the only time the dual diagram's outer ring catches no shadow. She kneels beside the stone. She places her hands at two specific points on the diagram — the two points where the opening and closing arcs intersect. She presses. The stone does not move. The earth around the stone moves.*
>
> *A square of soil and grass, two meters across, sinks downward perhaps fifteen centimeters. Then it rotates — the entire square, around the stone, ninety degrees — and beneath it appears a shaft. Stone-lined. Older than the chapel above. The shaft drops into darkness. The air rising from it smells of the same mineral edge as the burial pit, and of something else that has not been smelled in centuries: cold incense, made from a resin that does not grow in this climate.*

**Héctor explains, if asked**: *"Esto es lo que llaman La Salamanca. No es una sola — hay varias. Esta es una de las antiguas. Una de las que se hicieron antes de que los Jesuitas hicieran su capilla encima."* — *"This is what they call La Salamanca. It is not just one — there are several. This is one of the old ones. One of the ones that was made before the Jesuits built their chapel on top of it."*

This is the **entrance to La Salamanca** in the overview's vocabulary. The shaft drops to the **bone chamber** below.

### NEW SCENE — EL POMBERO RETURNS

As the players prepare to descend, El Pombero is sitting on a wall of the ruins. He has not been seen since C4. He is whittling a stick. He says, without looking up:

> *"Abajo se baja con preguntas, no con armas. Si llevan armas, igual bajen. Pero no van a servir."*  — *"You go down with questions, not weapons. If you carry weapons, go down anyway. They won't be useful."*

If asked what is below: *"Lo viejo. Lo que estuvo antes de los curas, antes de los charrúas, antes de los tehuelches. La gente que vino primero. No queda nadie de ellos. Pero la geometría queda."*  — *"The old. What was before the priests, before the Charrúa, before the Tehuelche. The first people. None of them are left. But the geometry remains."*

He warns them about three things in the descent — all useful, none specific:
1. **The bone chamber** at the bottom of the shaft contains the dead of the previous closing attempt. Do not disturb the alignment of the bones; they are still doing work.
2. **The whispering tunnels** beyond the bone chamber are not haunted, but they *amplify*. Every whisper one of them makes will be heard by all the others, in their own voices. Speak carefully.
3. **The Great Wizard's chamber** at the deepest point is closed and has been closed for many decades. The players should not seek it this chapter. *"No están listos. Yo tampoco."* — *"You are not ready. Neither am I."*

He vanishes between two glances. Marta says: *"He has been here longer than the carving. Don't ask how."*

### NEW SCENE — THE BONE CHAMBER

Run this as a brief but vivid descent scene. The shaft drops twelve meters. The chamber at the bottom is roughly six meters across, hexagonal, the walls of the same imported limestone as the Saens stone above. The ceiling is low — the players bend slightly. The floor is **bones, in the dual diagram's pattern**.

> *The bones are arranged into the complete dual diagram, at chamber-floor scale. There are perhaps two hundred individual bones — long bones, skulls, vertebrae, intermixed deliberately by hand. The skulls are placed at the diagram's outer ring positions. They face inward. They are not human. The skulls are too long, too narrow at the jaw, the eye sockets are too far apart. They are not from a species the players have a name for.*
>
> *In the very center of the diagram, where Nahuel will eventually stand at the closing, is a single human skull. Old. Modern human. Its forehead is unscarred. **It is Padre Ignacio Saens.** He came back here, alone, the year before he died, and laid himself on the diagram in place of the people he didn't have — opener, knowledge-holder, and counter-figure all at once — and bought what time he could. His body was taken into the geometry as substitute. The skull is the only piece left because the geometry needs a center, and a man's bone holds where parchment doesn't.*

**Keeper context — what Saens actually achieved**: This is the visual evidence of A4 / page 79: *"I could not close it alone."* The closing requires three roles (opener, knowledge-holder, counter-figure). Saens had the manuscript, had Antu-Rayo's instruction, had no successors, and no opener to anchor — so he stood in for *all* of it himself. What he produced was a **partial binding** that held for ~6 decades, sustained afterward by Antu-Rayo's lineage carrying counter-rites forward (Kuyen's grandmother, Rosa's elders). When that lineage thinned in the *campañas al desierto*, the partial seal began to fail. Don Eusebio's ritual eight months ago did not *break* Saens's work — it widened a wound already opening. **The bone chamber is not a finished closing. It is the cost of a brave, incomplete one.** Players who connect this to Page 79 understand what *they* will be paying for if they try the same shortcut at the pit.

**SAN Check**: 1/1D6 — for what Saens did, alone, ahead of the players, knowing it would not be enough.

**A Hard Cthulhu Mythos roll** on the non-human skulls: *they are "the first people" El Pombero referenced.* They are not human. They are the species that carved the original geometry, before any human population reached the Pampas. The closing tradition descends from them, transmitted via the Tehuelche who learned it from the *first people* during a brief contact period, then transmitted to Saens via Antu-Rayo, then to Kuyen via her grandmother. Each transmission has lost detail. The full closing was never available to humans alone.

This is the campaign's deepest revelation about the binding's origin. It is also the strongest argument the players will hear that **the Necronomicón may contain the full ritual** — because the *first people*'s knowledge survived in writing somewhere, and the most likely place is Buenos Aires.

### NEW SCENE — THE WHISPERING TUNNELS

Beyond the bone chamber, three tunnels lead deeper. They are roughly two meters tall, smooth-walled, their ceilings carved with the dual diagram's outer ring repeated at one-meter intervals. **The whispering happens** because the tunnels are *acoustically tuned* — every word spoken inside any tunnel is repeated at the entrance of all three tunnels, in the speaker's voice, at the exact volume of the original.

Practical effect at the table: any roll that involves speaking in the tunnels is at one step of difficulty harder, because the speaker hears their own words returned to them in real time. **A Listen roll** identifies the tuning — the geometry of the carved diagrams is what produces the effect, and a player who recognizes this can use silence as a navigational tool.

The three tunnels lead to:
- **A small chamber with a stone table** holding offerings: a cup, a bone needle, a clay tablet inscribed in a script the players cannot read. **Hard Library Use** identifies it as **proto-Tehuelche**, but the script is older than known proto-Tehuelche by perhaps a thousand years. The clay tablet is one of the campaign's permanent objects. Mercedes will want to see it.
- **A flooded chamber** where the floor has dropped and groundwater has filled it to chest height. Submerged in the water, visible by lantern: the remains of *another* dual diagram, same scale as the chapel stone. Underwater the geometry is intact and *still active* — a witness-stone (overview, Arc 4) lowered here would read warm.
- **A sealed door** at the end of the third tunnel. The Great Wizard's chamber. El Pombero warned. The players should leave it sealed this chapter. If they force it: this jumps directly to C6 content with no time for setup. The Keeper can permit it, but warn the players first.

### Observable consequences (D5)

- **At the Saens stone, before descent**: A player who places a half-full canteen on the carved circle will see the water's surface form *standing waves* in the dual-diagram pattern — the fluid is reading the geometry the same way iron filings read a magnet. The waves persist as long as the canteen sits there. No roll. Show the SVG handout next to the canteen.
- **In the bone chamber**: Lanterns brighten by about 30% in the chamber's center (where the human skull lies) and dim by about 30% at the outer ring. There is no draft. Investigators carrying lit cigars will notice the smoke draws inward toward the central skull rather than rising — the geometry is breathing.
- **In the whispering tunnels**: A Marked PC speaking quietly hears their own words returned in their own voice — but with a *second* voice underneath, faint, in the rhythm of the dual diagram's outer ring. (Twelve syllables, then a pause, then twelve syllables. They will recognize this rhythm in C8 at the closing, and in C12 at the dock.)
- **At the flooded chamber**: A handful of pampas grass dropped into the water *will not sink*. It floats and arranges itself into a small dual-diagram on the surface within thirty seconds. The water is not water-as-water; it is water as *geometry-saturated medium*.
