# SESSION 2 — LAS SEÑALES → LA CAPILLA
### *The Signs → The Chapel*

**Arc Goal**: Break the false premise → show the counter-ritual → tragedy (La Llorona)
**Emotional Arc**: Doubt → Disquiet → Grief
**Key Seed**: Not all rituals are the same. Someone was trying to stop this.

---

## OPENING SITUATION

The investigators have returned from the south pasture with questions Don Eusebio cannot answer satisfactorily. The tracks do not match any indigenous raiding pattern. The geometry is too precise. Something doesn't fit.

This session begins with the investigators following the physical trail — or their own suspicions — east, away from the estancia. The false path leads somewhere Don Eusebio did not intend: to San Ruiz, to a burned chapel, and to a ghost who has been waiting for someone to listen.

**If investigators return to Don Eusebio first**: He will be more careful now. He senses they are close to something. He will add an additional payment to redirect them toward a specific indigenous camp "three hours east." The camp is real — Kuyen's people are there — but his framing of it is entirely false. Let investigators go there if they want; they will find the camp empty (the tribe has moved for the season) and the tracks leading to San Ruiz instead.

**If investigators go directly east**: The tracks from the pit's eastern rim are old but present. They follow the pattern — looping, stopping, restarting — but always trending east. Eventually the ground changes: harder, caliche-rich. The tracks become intermittent. Then: a road. The road leads to San Ruiz.

---

## THE WORLD: SAN RUIZ

**Setting**: San Ruiz is — was — a small farming settlement, perhaps forty years old. Founded by a mix of Criollo settlers and converted Tehuelche families who came in under the missions, it had perhaps twelve households at its peak. Adobe houses, a communal well, an orchard of peach trees now wild and leggy, a small chapel on the east end of the settlement.

> *The village is still. Not abandoned-still, not ghost-town-still — something between. The houses are intact. Doors are open. There is bread on a table in one window, hard as stone. Washing on a line, faded to gray. The orchard grows uncontrolled into the road. There is no smell of decay. No bodies. No bones in the street. Just — a place that was left, quickly, while things were being used.*
>
> *A goat lives in the communal well housing. It watches you with horizontal pupils and chews.*

**Keeper Notes**:
- San Ruiz was evacuated approximately 14 months ago. The departure was orderly enough to take people and animals but not belongings. They left mid-meal, mid-task.
- There are marks on the *inside* of several doorframes — the same angular scratched symbols from the chapel, in miniature. Some families were trying to protect their homes.
- The symbols on the doorframes are the counter-symbol (the binding), not the opening diagram from Don Eusebio's pit. Even here, someone was fighting back.
- If investigators examine the bread: **Natural History or Chemistry** — the bread is not molded, despite being at least a year old. The air in San Ruiz is drier than it should be. Something about the land here resists organic process.

**Available Clues in the Village**:
| Location | Clue | Skill |
|---|---|---|
| Doorframes | Scratched binding symbols — multiple houses | Spot Hidden |
| Kitchen table | Preserved bread — unnatural lack of decay | Natural History / Chemistry |
| Main road | Two different shoe/foot types — departure was group, mixed ages, calm | Tracking |
| One house (northeast) | A child's drawing on the wall: a circle with lines and a stick figure standing outside it | Spot Hidden |
| The well | The water tastes faintly of minerals — same metallic note as the pit | Taste / Chemistry |

## NPC: ROSA

**Role**: The last witness. The keeper of what happened. A partial guide to truth.

**Appearance**: 71 years old. Mapuche-Tehuelche descent, short, wide-built, with a face like carved ironwood — deep lines, dark eyes that have looked at the world steadily for a long time. She wears a black dress, a wool shawl despite the heat, and a small silver ornament at her throat that is not quite a cross — four lines meeting at angles. Her hands are always busy: she is usually mending, grinding, or weaving. She works while she talks.

**Voice**: Low, measured. She does not hurry. She will wait through silences that make others uncomfortable. She speaks in a mix of Spanish and occasional Mapudungun words she does not translate — not to be obscure, but because some things, she says, do not exist in Spanish.

**Where She Is Found**: In the last house at the east end of the village, closest to the chapel. The door is open. She is grinding corn. She does not appear surprised to see strangers. *"I thought someone would come eventually,"* she says, without looking up. *"Sit down."*

**Why She Stayed**:
> *"Someone needs to remember. If I leave, it is as if it never happened. The land forgets faster than people do."*

She stayed also because she is too old to be afraid of what might come back, and because she believes — correctly — that her presence near the chapel's symbols helps maintain the partial binding she and others scratched there before leaving.

**Motivation**: To be heard, finally. To pass what she knows to someone who might act on it correctly. She does not trust easily, but she is not hostile. She is *tired*. The investigators are possibly the first people she has spoken to in months.

---

**Possible Questions & Answers — Rosa**

*"Where did everyone go?"*
> "Away. South, most of them. To Tres Lagunas, to family. Some east, to the tribe." *(She means Kuyen's people.)* "They went before it got worse. I told them to go sooner." *(She resumes grinding.)*

*"Why did they leave? What happened here?"*
> "It started as dreams. The same dream, many people — a bright place underground, something pulling upward. Then the animals. Then the children stopped sleeping." *(Pause.)* "Then people started going to the south pasture at night. Not running — walking. Calm. Like they were invited." *(She stops grinding.)* "Three went. Two came back wrong. One didn't come back."

*"What do you mean, 'came back wrong'?"*
> "They sat. They ate. They answered questions. But they didn't look at you the same way anymore. Like looking at a painting of a person instead of a person." *(She touches the silver ornament at her throat.)* "We sent them south with the others. I don't know what they are now."

*"Who is responsible for this?"*
> "The land didn't do this by itself. Someone called." *(She looks at you directly for the first time.)* "Someone drew the shape. The shape calls the thing. The thing answers." *(She will not say Don Eusebio's name. Not because she doesn't know — she may — but because naming the guilty is not her role here. Finding the shape is.)*

*"What are the marks on the doorframes?"*
> "Warding. Old Mapuche, old Tehuelche mixed — we didn't always agree on the words but we agreed on the need." *(She pauses.)* "Not enough. The shape was too complete by then. Like trying to dam a river with your hands after the rain."

*"What is in the chapel?"*
> *(She is still for a moment.)* "What was in the chapel." *(She resumes her work.)* "Go see. But go during the day. And don't — whatever you hear there, don't answer it. She's not speaking to you."

*"Who is 'she'?"*
> "Dolores. She lived here. She understood what was happening before any of us." *(Pause.)* "She tried to stop it alone. You can see what she scratched over the old symbols. She worked for three days. Then she..." *(Rosa does not finish. She looks at the door, at the direction of the chapel.)* "Go see."

*"Are the tribes responsible for the cattle deaths?"*
> *(A long silence. Then:)* "The Valdez man told you that." *(It is not a question.)* "No. Kuyen's people have been trying to close what he opened. They've been conducting the binding since before the cattle started dying." *(She sets down her grinding stone.)* "You've been lied to."

*"What should we do?"*
> "Find Kuyen. Don't interrupt the ritual. Whatever you see them doing — let them finish." *(She looks at you with absolute seriousness.)* "I need you to understand this before you go: the thing that was called is not yet fully here. The rituals are keeping it at the edge. If the binding is broken — if someone interrupts it — it comes through. And then there is nothing between it and everything."

**Roleplaying Hook**: Rosa will not give all of this information in one sitting. The first meeting she gives the essentials: people left, something was called, the chapel matters. If investigators ask respectfully and listen — actually listen, do not rush — she gives more. If they dismiss her or treat her as superstitious, she gives them the minimum and sends them to the chapel.

**Rosa's Hidden Knowledge**: She knows the complete binding symbol. She scratched it from memory on the floor beneath her corn-grinding stone. She will share it only if investigators have proven themselves trustworthy — likely only if they survive the chapel encounter and return to her.

## SCENE 2 — THE CHAPEL OF SAN RUIZ

### Setting Description

> *The chapel stands at the east end of the village road, its back almost against the old fence line. It was small to begin with — four walls, a wooden roof that half-collapsed in the fire, a single arched doorframe still standing. The fire burned hot; the walls are scorched black in stripes above the window openings. The wooden cross above the door is ash. But the stone walls are intact, and they are covered.*
>
> *On every interior wall surface — two layers. First, carved into the stone itself: deep, clean lines, geometric and precise, incorporating what might be Catholic iconography (crosses, fish forms) interwoven with angular Mapuche patterns. These are old. The carving took skill and time. This was a community project.*
>
> *Over them — scratched in the soot and plaster, frantic, hurried, with something sharp — a second set of marks. The same geometry, but reversed. Inverted. Where the carved symbols reach outward, the scratched ones close inward. Where one opens, the other seals.*

**Keeper Notes**:
- The carved symbols (original) are the *opening diagram* — the same geometry as Don Eusebio's pit, rendered in devotional form. The community had been incorporating it into their religious practice for years, unknowingly reinforcing it. A **Cthulhu Mythos or Occult (Hard)** roll: this pattern predates the chapel. It was here before the building. The community built the chapel *on* it.
- The scratched symbols (counter) are Dolores's work. She recognized the opening diagram for what it was and spent three days inverting every symbol she could reach. Her work is imperfect — she ran out of time — but it disrupted the pattern enough to slow the acceleration. These are the binding symbols, the same ones on Rosa's doorframes, on Tomás's stone (reverse side, if players examine it carefully).
- A **Spot Hidden** in the corner behind the collapsed altar: Dolores's tool. A sharpened piece of iron gate railing, worn smooth at the grip end from hours of use. On its flat side, scratched in tiny letters: *"no alcancé la mitad"* — "I didn't finish half."

**SAN Check**: 0/1 — the chapel itself, the weight of what it represents, the two layers of symbols telling a story of attempted salvation.

---

### Examining the Symbols

**Skill checks and what they reveal**:

| Skill | Result |
|---|---|
| Occult | The carved symbols are ritual geometry — their purpose is to draw something toward a fixed point. The scratched symbols attempt to reverse this. The conflict between them creates interference — it weakened the original but didn't stop it. |
| Art/Craft (Drawing) or Architecture | The carved symbols are extremely precise. The spacing and angles are mathematically consistent. The scratched symbols are approximate — the work of someone who understood the *intention* but was working from memory and instinct, not training. |
| History | The carved foundation symbols are older than the chapel's construction (approx. 1845). They might be colonial-era. Or older. Much older. |
| Library Use (against recovered documents) | If investigators have the manuscript fragment (acquired later from Don Eusebio's study), the carved symbols match its diagrams exactly. |
| Cthulhu Mythos | The geometry is not religious. It is dimensional. The opening diagram creates a sustained weakness in spatial geometry — a path. The binding closes the path. Both the Mapuche patterning and the Catholic iconography are overlay — the core symbol is something neither tradition created. |

**The Fragment**: In the rubble beneath the collapsed roofing, investigators with **Spot Hidden** find a piece of carved stone — broken from the wall, presumably in the fire or collapse. It shows a segment of the opening diagram combined with a partial binding overlay. Together with the hide and Tomás's stone, this is the third and most complete fragment. A player who has all three and makes an **INT** roll can now visualize roughly 75% of the complete diagram. The geometry is almost familiar. Like a shape seen in a dream.

## SCENE 3 — LA LLORONA

### The Entity: La Llorona

**What she is (for the Keeper only)**: La Llorona — The Weeping Woman — is one of Latin America's oldest and most persistent folkloric figures: a woman who lost her children (usually by her own hand in the original legend) and now wanders, weeping, searching. In your game, she is neither demon nor angel. She is a *remnant* — the psychic echo of **Dolores Quiroga**, a woman of San Ruiz who understood what was happening, acted with total commitment, and failed, and whose grief and determination left an impression in the ritual-charged space of the chapel so strong that something of her remained.

She is not conscious in a full human sense. She is an echo playing on repeat. She moves between the symbols she scratched, touching them, checking them, weeping. Occasionally a fragment of her original awareness surfaces — and in those moments, she can speak. What she says is not addressed to anyone in particular. It is what she was thinking when she died.

**Appearance**: A woman in her 40s, in a dark dress, dark hair loose. She is translucent — not invisible, but thin, like a figure seen through smoke. Her face, when visible, is not frightening. It is exhausted. Her eyes are closed most of the time. Her hands move constantly, touching the scratched symbols on the walls, checking each one.

**Sound**: Low, continuous weeping — not the theatrical wail of folklore but quiet, private weeping. The sound someone makes when they have been crying for a long time and the sound has become almost involuntary. Occasionally words are audible, in Spanish, half-whispered.

**Movement**: She moves between the scratched symbols in sequence, in the same order, always. She is checking her work. Every 3–4 minutes she pauses at the place where her tool ran out of reach (the high sections of the far wall) and her weeping intensifies briefly. Then continues.

**Behavior Sequence**:

1. **Entrance**: As investigators examine the symbols, the temperature drops noticeably — 4–5°C. The sound of weeping becomes audible before she is visible.
2. **Manifestation**: She appears at the far end of the chapel near the original carved altar location, moving toward the first scratched symbol.
3. **Ignoring the investigators**: She does not acknowledge them. She moves between her symbols. She is not performing for an audience.
4. **Moments of partial awareness**: At certain symbols — the ones she scratched with most urgency — she pauses, and speaks. These are not responses to questions. They are fragments of her last conscious moments:

> *"They didn't listen. I showed them the shapes and they said I was confused. Women are always confused."*

> *"The Valdez man — I saw him in the field. I saw what he was doing. He didn't know what he was doing. That is the worst kind."*

> *"You're too late. You were too late before you arrived. But that doesn't mean you stop."*

> *"Find Kuyen. She knows the full binding. Tell her — tell her the eastern wall is incomplete. I couldn't reach."*

5. **Reaction to the stone fragment**: If investigators place the carved stone fragment on the ground near her path, she stops. Kneels (or approximates kneeling — she doesn't quite interact with physical surfaces consistently). She touches the fragment. She says: *"Yes. Yes, this is the part I couldn't reach. Take it. Take it to Kuyen."* Then resumes her circuit.

6. **If provoked/touched**: If an investigator attempts to touch her, pass through her, or interact physically — she does not harm them. But the investigator feels a rush of emotion that is not theirs: exhaustion, determination, grief, and beneath it, a terror so controlled it has become almost calm. **SAN 1/1D6 if provoked**. The emotion fades within seconds.

7. **Hidden remains**: In the far corner, beneath the collapsed roof section and a fallen beam — visible only if investigators move the debris (STR roll or combined effort) — a shallow depression in the earth floor. In it: small bones. A child's, probably. And beside them, scratched into the clay below the floor, the binding symbol in its *complete* form, drawn with a finger in the dirt by someone who knew the end was coming and wanted to leave the answer where it might be found. **This is the key clue of Session 2.**

8. **Departure**: After approximately 10 minutes, she moves to the altar position — the center of her circuit — and fades. The temperature rises. The weeping stops. As she disappears, the ash on the floor briefly stirs into a circular shape — the same geometry — and settles.

**SAN Check**: 0/1D4 (sustained presence and weeping)

---

### Optional: The Child's Remains

The child's bones are small — approximately 4–6 years old. They are arranged with care. There is a small carved wooden animal (a horse or guanaco) beside them. There is no sign of violence. The child was placed here deliberately, in protection.

**If investigators investigate**: A **Medicine** roll — the child did not die violently. Likely illness or exposure. The burial is recent enough (within two years) to have occurred after the ritual disturbances began. Someone — Dolores, probably — chose this place as a last safe location for a child who died during the crisis.

**SAN Check** (if investigators examine): 0/1D3 — not horror, but sorrow.

**Roleplaying hook**: If an investigator has children, or has lost a child, the Keeper should note this scene quietly. No mechanical penalty — just a moment of human weight. The horror of this arc is not just cosmic. It is about what people do to protect the ones they love, and how often it isn't enough.

## END OF SESSION 2

**The key clue recovered**: The complete binding symbol — drawn by Dolores in the clay beneath the floor. With this, investigators (or Kuyen) have everything needed to complete the ritual.

**What players should now believe or know**:
- Don Eusebio opened this. Not the tribes.
- The tribes — specifically Kuyen's people — have been performing counter-rituals to close it.
- Dolores understood and tried to help. She failed alone.
- The binding symbol exists in complete form and can be used.
- Something is still incomplete — "the eastern wall."

**What players should NOT yet know**:
- What the entity actually is or looks like.
- That Nahuel is cracking under the strain.
- That Don Eusebio knows the investigators are getting close and is considering more drastic action.

## GM NOTES

**The emotional core**: This session is about grief and the tragedy of being right too late. Dolores knew. Rosa knew. The village was warned. Nothing was enough. Let the players feel the weight of that. Don't rush it.

**Empathy, not terror**: La Llorona should not be frightening. She should be heartbreaking. If players leave this scene unnerved by her sadness rather than her scariness, you have done your job. The horror comes later — from knowing that someone tried this hard and it still wasn't enough.

**Doubt before Session 3**: By the end of this session, investigators should be doubting everything Don Eusebio told them and beginning to feel the first edges of guilt — they were hired to stop the tribes, who are the only people actually helping. If they have reported back to Don Eusebio at any point and given him information about Kuyen's location, they should now realize they may have done something terrible.

**Rosa's final word**: If investigators return to Rosa after the chapel encounter, she will look at them without warmth but without hostility. *"Now you know what you're in the middle of."* She will share the complete binding symbol if they ask, confirming what Dolores left in the clay. *"You need to find Kuyen. And you need to let her finish."*

**Don Eusebio's move**: If investigators have been in contact with him, he is beginning to suspect they are no longer working toward his goal. He may send a rider — one of his hands, loyal out of debt, not knowledge — to intercept them before they reach the tribe. The rider's job is to bring them back for a "meeting." This is optional pressure, not a combat encounter. The rider doesn't know why he was sent. He just works there.
