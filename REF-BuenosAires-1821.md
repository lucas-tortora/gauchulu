# REF — BUENOS AIRES, 1821

*Keeper setting reference for Arc 4 (Chapters [10](chapter-10.md#chapter-10--la-llegada)–[12](chapter-12.md#chapter-12--el-libro)). Historically grounded, written to be run. Player-facing description in the chapters is Argentine Spanish; this doc is Keeper English. For the campaign's use of the city as a **containment geometry**, see [How the real city grounds the campaign](#how-the-real-city-grounds-the-campaign) at the bottom.*

---

## THE MOMENT — 1821 IN ONE BREATH

Five years after independence (1816), one year after the **Anarchy of 1820** (the province cycled through governors in a single day), Buenos Aires has just steadied. **Martín Rodríguez** governs the Province of Buenos Aires; his minister **Bernardino Rivadavia** is the engine — an Enlightenment reformer remaking the city by decree in what optimists call *la Feliz Experiencia*, "the Happy Experiment."

Everything is **one year old or about to change**:
- The **Universidad de Buenos Aires** was inaugurated **12 August 1821** in the old Jesuit block.
- The **Cabildo** — the colonial town council, three centuries old — is being **abolished** (its functions dissolved by the end of 1821).
- **Ecclesiastical reform** is imminent (enacted 1822): tithes suppressed, several religious orders dissolved, convents reduced. The Church is on the back foot for the first time in 240 years.
- A reorganized **municipal police** (the *vigilantes*, a *Jefatura de Policía*) is being stood up to replace the Cabildo's old ward-magistrates — new, thin, unsure of its authority.

This is a city where old institutions are being pulled down faster than new ones can set. **Things slip through the gap.** (That gap is where the Keepers operate — and where a death can go uninvestigated.)

Rosas and the terror are still eight years off. This is the brief, hopeful, reforming window before the *guerra civil* swallows everything.

## THE CITY AT A GLANCE

- **Population** ~55,000–60,000 in the city; a large town, not yet a metropolis. Walkable end to end in an hour.
- **Layout**: the **1580 grid** laid by Juan de Garay — the *traza*, eleven-odd blocks by nine of square *manzanas*, streets meeting at right angles, radiating from the **Plaza de la Victoria** and the river. Regular, rational, old. *(This is the "containment geometry" the Marked feel on entry — see below.)*
- **The river**: the **Río de la Plata**, brown with silt, enormous and **treacherously shallow** at the city's edge. There is **no port**. Ocean ships anchor kilometers out in the *rada*; passengers and cargo transfer to lighters, then to **high-wheeled oxcarts** that wade the final shallows — a famously undignified, muddy arrival. The small-craft harbor is the **Riachuelo** to the south (the *Boca*).
- **Water**: the river is undrinkable near shore. Households keep **cisterns** (*aljibes*) for rainwater; **water-carriers** (*aguateros*) sell river-water from carts, left to settle. *(This grounds every "well" / aljibe in the campaign — they are rain-cisterns, not deep wells.)*
- **Streets**: mostly **packed dirt** — dust in the *seca*, shin-deep mud in rain; a few central blocks newly cobbled. Numbered lots, named streets (**Florida, Perú, Defensa, Reconquista, Belgrano, Victoria** — all real, all used in the chapters).
- **Light**: **candles and whale-/tallow-oil lamps** (*faroles*). Public street-lighting is minimal and new. Nights are genuinely dark.

## THE MAP — LANDMARKS THE PARTY WILL CROSS

- **Plaza de la Victoria / Plaza de Mayo** — the civic heart, split by the arcaded market **Recova Vieja** (1803). The **Pirámide de Mayo** (1811) stands in it.
- **The Fort** (*el Fuerte*) — the riverside fortress on the plaza's east side; **seat of government**, the governor's residence. (Later demolished for the Casa Rosada.)
- **The Cabildo** — west side of the plaza; the town-council building, its authority draining away this very year.
- **The Cathedral** — north side; its great neoclassical façade still under construction.
- **La Manzana de las Luces** — "the Block of Enlightenment," the old Jesuit block (Perú/Bolívar/Moreno/Alsina). Holds the **church of San Ignacio** (the city's oldest, 1710s–30s), the colonial *Colegio*, the **Biblioteca Pública** (public library, 1810), the **Procuraduría**, and the celebrated **colonial tunnels** beneath — real, documented, and still a live local legend in 1821: popularly said to connect the Manzana to the Cabildo, the old Fuerte, and the convents of La Merced and San Francisco, dug or widened by the Jesuits before their 1767 expulsion, reused (by some accounts) during the English invasions of 1806–07. Nobody in 1821 has a complete map of them. **The new University is seated here.** *(The campaign's sealed 1610 vault sits under this block — a single walled-off spur of the same network. See [C10](chapter-10.md#the-university-public-face) for the legend surfacing and [C11](chapter-11.md#the-vault-finally) for the tunnel scene.)*
- **The Riachuelo / la Boca** — the southern creek-harbor: **saladeros** (salt-meat plants) and **curtiembres** (tanneries) line it, reeking of brine, blood, lime, and quebracho. *(The Cursed Gaucho's tannery and the C12 dock are here.)*
- **Recoleta** — to the north: the Franciscan *Recoletos* convent and the church of **Nuestra Señora del Pilar** (1732). Their orchard is about to become the **Cementerio del Norte** (Recoleta cemetery), inaugurated **1822** — brand-new, barely open, when the campaign ends.
- **Retiro / Monserrat** — northern plaza (a former slave-depot site) and the southern barrio *Monserrat*, the **"barrio del Tambor,"** heart of the Afro-Argentine community and its candombe drumming.
- **The Alameda** — the riverside promenade along the *bajo*, where the respectable stroll at dusk.

## DAILY LIFE & THE SENSES

Run the city through the nose and the ear before the eye.

- **Smell**: leather curing and rendering tallow from the *saladeros*; the brackish river; horse and oxcart; woodsmoke; *mate*.
- **Sound**: **church bells** arguing with the **Cabildo tower clock**; oxcart wheels shrieking on axles; the **serenos** (night watchmen) calling the hour and weather block by block — *"Las once han dado, y sereno…"*; drumming from Monserrat on feast nights.
- **Rhythm**: an early city. Business at dawn, a long **siesta** in the heat, work again to dusk, then *tertulias* (evening gatherings) behind shuttered windows. After the serenos call the late hours, the streets **empty**.
- **Trade**: the economy is **hides and salt-beef** (*tasajo* — cheap salted meat shipped to feed enslaved workers in Brazil and Cuba). The *saladero* is the engine; the *estanciero* and the merchant are the money.
- **Everyday things**: *pulperías* (grocery-bar-general stores, the social hub); *mate* passed hand to hand; **silver** coin (*pesos* and *reales*, 8 reales to the peso — Potosí silver; the first bank opens 1822); *carretas* (oxcarts) and, for the road, the *galera* / *diligencia* (stagecoach).

## WHO LIVES HERE

- **Criollos** — the Spanish-descended *porteños*, from the merchant and landowning families down to laborers.
- **Afro-Argentines** — a very large presence (historically up to a third of the city; still substantial in the 1820s), both free and enslaved. **Slavery is not abolished** — but the 1813 *Libertad de Vientres* freed children born after 1813, and Afro-Argentine militias, artisans, and the *candombe* societies of Monserrat are woven through city life.
- **Gauchos & country people** — visible at the edges: horse-people from the *campaña*, out of place on cobbles, watched with unease. *(Your PCs read as exactly this — armed frontier men in a reforming port that no longer knows what to do with them.)*
- **Foreigners** — a growing **British and French** merchant colony since independence; Genoese and Basque sailors and traders on the river; the odd Italian, Irish, North American. Buenos Aires is opening to the Atlantic and knows it.
- **Indigenous peoples** — beyond the frontier, not in the city; their land is the *desierto* the *campañas* are pushing into.

## LAW, ORDER & THE NIGHT

- Policing is in transition: the Cabildo's old *alcaldes de barrio* (ward magistrates) are giving way to Rivadavia's new **Jefatura de Policía** and its *vigilantes* and *serenos*. In practice the city is **lightly policed**, especially at night and at the edges.
- A death among the powerful (a **Keeper** like Saráchaga) draws the *vigilantes urbanos* — but they are new, deferential, and out of their depth; **the Keepers investigate their own**, and no magistrate will pursue a wealthy man like **Lacasa** to his *estancia*. This is why the campaign's murder can be solved by the players and never by the state.
- **The river is the exit.** Anyone who wants to move something out of the country moves it down the **Riachuelo** to a ship in the roads — exactly the C12 transport.

## GETTING WORD IN AND OUT

- **No railroad, no telegraph.** News travels at the speed of a horse. Overland mail runs the **postas** (relay stations with fresh mounts along the post roads) — days to the interior, a week-plus to the cordillera.
- Across the estuary lies **Colonia del Sacramento** (Banda Oriental) and, beyond, Montevideo — a day or two by sail; the Keeper-allied chapter house and the brig's destination.
- A letter from the pampas reaches the city in **days**; a Keeper relay with fresh mounts, faster. *(This is why Saúl's news, Mercedes' letters, and the Coypac report all arrive on the delays the chapters specify.)*

## HOW THE REAL CITY GROUNDS THE CAMPAIGN

The history is not decoration — every supernatural beat rests on a real feature of 1821 Buenos Aires:

- **The grid *is* the geometry.** Garay's rational 1580 *traza* is exactly the kind of deliberate spatial order the campaign's binding-geometry uses. The Marked feel the city as a **city-scale containment diagram** because, in the fiction, that is what someone made it.
- **The Manzana de las Luces = the vault.** A real block of Jesuit learning, with real **tunnels** beneath it, sitting at the civic center — the perfect place to have sealed a book in 1610 and to hide it under a brand-new university in 1821.
- **Reform = exposure.** Rivadavia tearing down the Cabildo and curbing the Church means the old guardians of the block are losing their cover and their authority precisely as the containment weakens. The Keepers are improvising in the gap.
- **The saladero coast = the dock and the tannery.** The Riachuelo's real salt-and-hide industry gives the C11 tannery and the C12 transport their smell, their geography, and their traffic (a chest on a launch to a brig is unremarkable there).
- **A city that no longer knows your PCs.** Frontier gauchos in a reforming Atlantic port are conspicuous and unwelcome — good for paranoia, bad for blending in.
- **The muddy, portless river** makes the transport slow and exposed (carts, lighters, a long dock) — which is exactly the vulnerability El Patrón engineered.

## PERIOD-ACCURACY NOTES *(soften or ignore to taste)*

The chapters are already strongly period-grounded. Two small anachronisms, flagged so you can adjust on the fly if a history-minded player notices — neither needs a rewrite:

- **"Gas-lamps"** (a C12 atmospheric line): gas street-lighting did not reach Buenos Aires until **the 1850s**. In 1821 read it as **oil/whale-oil *faroles*** guttering, or candlelight in windows.
- **Recoleta cemetery**: the *Cementerio del Norte* (Recoleta) was inaugurated in **1822**, roughly a year after these events. If a body is "buried at Recoleta," treat the cemetery as **just opening** — or use the adjacent Recoleta church/convent ground instead.
- Everything else the chapters use — *Manzana de las Luces*, *vigilantes urbanos*, *Imprenta de los Expósitos*, the Riachuelo *saladeros*, *aljibes*, *serenos*, *galera*, the real street names — is period-correct. Lean into it.
